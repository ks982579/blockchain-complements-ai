## Overview

This paper presents EAIBS-PCOS, an integrated system that combines Hyperledger Fabric blockchain, machine learning (ML), and Explainable AI (XAI) for the secure storage and prediction of Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS), a hormonal disorder affecting an estimated 8-13% of women of reproductive age, with up to 70% of cases going undiagnosed. The work addresses two intertwined problems in healthcare AI: the lack of secure, interoperable, and privacy-preserving storage for sensitive patient medical records, and the "black box" opacity of ML-based diagnostic models that undermines clinician trust and runs afoul of regulations such as the EU's restrictions on automated decision-making. The proposed architecture uses a permissioned Hyperledger Fabric network (with seven stakeholder types - patients, physicians, insurers, health centers, researchers, pharmacies, and public health agencies) to immutably store patient records, while an ensemble ML model (Random Forest + XGBoost) performs PCOS prediction and LIME/SHAP techniques provide local and global explanations for each prediction. The system is evaluated on an open Kaggle dataset of 541 patients (41 attributes), achieving 98.04% accuracy, 99.5% precision, 96.5% recall, and 97.9% F1-score (10-fold cross-validation), and the blockchain layer is benchmarked with Hyperledger Caliper across throughput, latency, and execution time under varying transaction rates, worker counts, and transaction volumes. The paper's main contribution to the field is presenting what it claims is the first system to jointly evaluate blockchain performance metrics and ML/XAI performance metrics for PCOS diagnosis, positioning it as a blueprint for secure, transparent, and scalable predictive healthcare platforms.

## Background

The paper situates its motivation within a broader narrative (drawing on Alanazi, 2022; Permana, Leslie & Perdana, 2023; Rubinger et al., 2023; Goodman & Flaxman, 2016; WHO, 2019) that PCOS is widespread, underdiagnosed, and that ML/blockchain integration is poised to improve women's reproductive health outcomes. It draws on prior characterizations of ML's three categories (supervised, unsupervised, reinforcement) and its established healthcare applications such as disease detection, medical imaging diagnosis, and personalized treatment (citing Adadi & Berrada, 2020; Abouhawwash et al., 2023; Alamoudi et al., 2023). The "black box" problem in clinical AI and the regulatory push for explainability are framed using the EU's 2018 restrictions on automated individual decision-making (Goodman & Flaxman, 2016) and calls for AI transparency (Hoffman et al., 2018), alongside market-growth projections for XAI (NMSC, 2025) and surveys of XAI techniques such as LIME, SHAP, PDP, ICE, ALE, and PFI (Mohanty & Mishra, 2022; Saraswat et al., 2022). On the blockchain side, the paper builds on prior claims that blockchain's immutability, decentralization, and security address core healthcare data-management issues - interoperability, data sharing, and privacy (Hasselgren et al., 2020; Zaabar et al., 2021; Saeed et al., 2022; Wahyuni et al., 2024; Haleem et al., 2021) - and that Hyperledger Fabric specifically has proven effective for EHR management, consent management, and supply-chain applications (Kasyapa & Vanmathi, 2024). It reviews a body of prior PCOS-detection studies using ML/deep learning (Subha et al., 2024; Danaei Mehr & Polat, 2022; Tiwari et al., 2022; Ravishankar et al., 2023; Munjal & Khandia, 2020; Nandipati, Chew & Wah, 2020; Kavitha et al., 2023; Suganya et al., 2023; Vikas, Radhika & Vineesha, 2021; Lv et al., 2022) and a separate body of blockchain-ML integration studies in other health domains (Hu & Kar, 2024 on health monitoring with ModelChain; Sharma & Rohilla, 2023 on drug discovery; Sasikumar & Karthikeyan, 2023 on cardiovascular severity via Hyperledger; Heidari et al., 2023 on federated learning for lung cancer; Pandey et al., 2022 on waste recommendation; Iqbal et al., 2021 on veterinary data; Bharimalla et al., 2021 on NLP-based EHR with Hyperledger). The dataset itself originates from a third party (Kottarathil, 2020 on Kaggle, 541 patients from Kerala hospitals, 41 attributes). Evaluation methodology draws on established metric definitions for classification (Ferri, Hernández-Orallo & Modroiu, 2009; Hossin & Sulaiman, 2015; Tharwat, 2018) and blockchain performance (Hyperledger Blockchain Performance Metrics White Paper, 2018; Pajooh et al., 2022; Malik et al., 2023), as well as the foundational XAI algorithms LIME (Ribeiro, Singh & Guestrin, 2016) and SHAP (Lundberg & Lee, 2017).

## Key Points

- The paper identifies a literature gap: no prior work jointly evaluates both blockchain performance metrics (throughput, latency, resource consumption) and ML/XAI performance metrics (accuracy, precision, recall, F1) within a single integrated PCOS diagnosis system.
- It proposes EAIBS-PCOS, a novel architecture using a permissioned Hyperledger Fabric blockchain with seven defined stakeholder roles (patient, physician, insurer, health center, researcher, pharmacy, public health agency), each with specific access privileges, to ensure immutable and confidential storage of PCOS patient records.
- The paper formalizes the PCOS detection problem mathematically as an ensemble of Random Forest and XGBoost outputs (Eq. 1), combined with an XAI explanation function (Eq. 2) and a Hyperledger Fabric storage abstraction HF_B = (Ti, Ci, Li), with the dual research goal of maximizing both blockchain performance and model performance while minimizing prediction loss.
- It provides an example chaincode algorithm ("Update_Patient_details") that demonstrates how patient records can be updated within the Fabric network subject to physician access permissions granted by patients.
- The paper evaluates five candidate ML algorithms (SVM, KNN, Random Forest, XGBoost, Naive Bayes) on a preprocessed and SMOTE-balanced 541-patient PCOS dataset, and finds Random Forest and XGBoost perform best individually, motivating their ensembling.
- The resulting RF+XGBoost ensemble model achieves 98.04% accuracy, 99.5% precision, 96.5% recall, and 97.9% F1-score under 10-fold cross-validation (with the abstract separately reporting 98% accuracy, 100% precision, 98.04% recall, 99.01% F1-score), with a standard deviation of 0.01 across runs, and training/testing times of 1.38s and 0.01s respectively, supporting real-time applicability.
- The paper integrates SHAP (force plots) and LIME (local feature-contribution explanations) on top of the ensemble model to provide both global and per-instance interpretability of PCOS predictions for clinicians.
- Using Hyperledger Caliper's fixed-rate controller, the paper benchmarks the blockchain network across three experimental dimensions - transactions per second (tps), number of workers, and transaction count (TxNum) - and identifies optimized network parameters: tps of 125, 100 workers, and TxNum of 500, balancing throughput, latency, and execution time.
- The paper claims its system outperforms prior PCOS-detection studies (Tiwari et al., 2022; Subha et al., 2024; Rahman et al., 2024; Silambarasan, Nirmala & Mishra, 2024; Hassan & Mirza, 2020) on accuracy, precision, and F1-score, and asserts it is the first PCOS detection work to integrate both blockchain and XAI for combined security and transparency.

## Conclusion

The paper concludes that the EAIBS-PCOS system successfully demonstrates that blockchain (Hyperledger Fabric), ensemble ML (RF + XGBoost), and XAI (LIME and SHAP) can be combined into a single framework that secures patient data, delivers high-accuracy PCOS predictions, and provides interpretable explanations for clinical decision-making - meeting the study's stated goals of security, scalability, transparency, and interpretability. The quantitative results (reported as up to 99.03% accuracy, 100% precision, 98.04% recall in the conclusion) support the claim that the integrated approach is both accurate and computationally efficient enough for real-time use. However, the authors explicitly acknowledge several limitations: the model has not been validated on a real-time or larger dataset; the 541-patient sample may lack demographic diversity and could suffer from data quality issues affecting generalizability; the blockchain system's performance degrades with higher transaction volumes, raising scalability concerns; legal, regulatory, and ethical questions around deploying blockchain-based health systems remain unresolved; and the study focuses narrowly on detection without addressing treatment outcomes or long-term disease management. The authors propose future work directions including optimizing blockchain scalability, extending the framework to other diseases beyond PCOS, and testing real-world clinical deployment - framing these as the key open research questions for broader adoption of integrated blockchain-XAI healthcare systems.
