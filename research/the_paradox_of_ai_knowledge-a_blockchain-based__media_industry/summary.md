## Overview

This empirical-plus-design paper (Wu & Cai, MDPI *Future Internet*, 2025) studies how Chinese new media professionals perceive and intend to adopt AI text-to-video systems (exemplified by OpenAI's Sora), and uses those behavioral findings to motivate a blockchain-based decentralized governance framework. Operationalizing the Orientation–Stimulus–Orientation–Response (O-S-O-R) model, the authors survey 209 currently employed Chinese new media workers (collected April–May 2024 via snowball sampling) and fit a PLS-SEM model in SmartPLS 4.0 to test seven hypotheses plus two research questions linking interest in science/technology, social media information elaboration, AI knowledge confidence, risk and benefit perception, objective AI knowledge, and adoption intention. Their central empirical contribution is a "knowledge paradox": objective AI knowledge *negatively* moderates the effect of information elaboration on knowledge confidence (β = −0.251) and benefit perception (β = −0.135), meaning that better-informed professionals who engage more deeply with available (centralized) information become *more* skeptical and less confident, not more. The paper argues this paradox is evidence that centralized information ecosystems systematically exclude/misweight expert perspectives, and on that basis proposes a multi-tiered DAO governance architecture with five integrated mechanisms (Expert Assessment DAOs, Community Validation DAOs, real-time algorithm monitoring, professional integrity protection, and cross-border coordination), backed by concrete smart-contract patterns, a multi-token economic design, and a platform-selection analysis (Ethereum, Layer-2, Polkadot). It positions itself as empirical grounding for Web3-enabled AI governance.

## Background

The paper builds heavily on prior work it cites to frame both its behavioral and governance arguments. On the behavioral side, it draws on the O-S-O-R model from media/communication psychology (Markus & Zajonc; Jiang), where pre-orientation O1, stimulus S, post-orientation O2, and response R structure how mediated information shapes behavioral intention; it notes O-S-O-R has been "less frequently applied" to technology adoption. It cites prior technology-adoption literature showing risk and benefit perception are key, often negatively correlated, predictors of acceptance (with benefit perception typically outweighing risk), and that subjective knowledge *confidence* can drive behavior more than objective knowledge (e.g., overconfidence in detecting deepfakes). It flags that prior findings on AI knowledge and adoption are inconsistent (positive for autonomous-vehicle willingness; null for physicians' AI adoption), attributing this to heterogeneous measurement, domain specificity, and neglected mediation/moderation — motivating its own moderation test. For context it cites Chinese industry statistics (CNNIC/State Council: 1.074 billion online audiovisual users by Dec 2023, 98.3% of internet users; 151 min/day on short-video; ~80 million videos uploaded daily; 15.08 million full-time livestreamers) and the 2023 Hollywood writers'/actors' strike as evidence of AI labor disruption. On the governance side, the paper says (citing others) that traditional regulation suffers regulatory lag, jurisdictional limits, and technical-expertise gaps; self-regulation has conflicts of interest and no binding enforcement; and multistakeholder bodies face decision paralysis and capture. It cites blockchain/DAO literature (Hassan & De Filippi, Wright & De Filippi, Buterin, Szabo, Werbach, Beck et al.) for algorithmic transparency, distributed consensus, programmable smart-contract enforcement, tokenomics, and cross-border coordination. Importantly, it also cites counter-evidence about DAO limitations: The DAO 2016 hack ($60M via reentrancy), Polkadot governance participation below 15% with whale dominance, MakerDAO top-10 addresses controlling >50% of voting power, plus the oracle problem, gas/scalability costs, plutocracy, Sybil attacks, and privacy trade-offs. It connects its design to COVID-era organizational-resilience studies it cites, framing DAOs as a way to institutionalize crisis-driven ICT adaptations.

## Key Points

- The paper identifies a "knowledge paradox": objective AI knowledge negatively moderates the path from social media information elaboration to AI knowledge confidence (AIK × SMIE → AIcon, β = −0.251, p < 0.001) and to benefit perception (β = −0.135, p < 0.05); the moderation on risk perception was non-significant (β = −0.067, p = 0.501). Without the moderator these elaboration paths are strongly positive, so high-knowledge professionals who elaborate more become less confident and less optimistic.
- Interest in science and technology strongly and positively predicts social media information elaboration about Sora (IST → SMIE, β = 0.600, p < 0.001) — H1 supported.
- Information elaboration positively predicts confidence in AI knowledge (β = 0.294), benefit perception (β = 0.486), and risk perception (β = 0.231, p < 0.05) — H2, H3 (per the paper's framing), and H4 supported.
- Confidence in AI knowledge (β = 0.169, p < 0.001) and benefit perception (β = 0.051, p < 0.001, f² = 0.854, the dominant driver) positively predict intention to adopt Sora — H5 and H7 supported.
- Risk perception does NOT significantly predict adoption intention (β = 0.068, p = 0.942) — H6 not supported; the authors interpret this as risk perception being effectively discounted by new media workers.
- Among demographics (RQ2), only age has a significant (small) positive effect on adoption intention (β = 0.106, p < 0.05); education, gender, annual income, and industry tenure are all non-significant.
- The structural model explains 58% of variance in intention to adopt Sora (R² = 0.580), with good fit (SRMR = 0.080, GoF = 0.488) and adequate measurement properties (loadings 0.722–0.966 after dropping AIcon items 5 and 9; α/CR within thresholds; HTMT < 0.90).
- The paper contributes a novel application of the O-S-O-R behavioral model to blockchain/DAO governance design, mapping O1/S/O2/R onto token-holder roles, stimuli/audit events, collective intelligence formation, and on-chain voting.
- It proposes five integrated blockchain governance mechanisms: (1) Expert Assessment DAOs that weight expert skepticism via expertise-gated tokens, (2) Community Validation DAOs for broad stakeholder input, (3) real-time algorithm-monitoring/audit DAOs (bias detection, misinformation verification, transparency requirements), (4) blockchain-based professional integrity protection (reputation/credibility tokens, peer review, accountability smart contracts), and (5) cross-border coordination DAOs for international harmonization.
- It specifies a multi-tiered DAO architecture and a multi-token economic design (EXPERT, COMMUNITY, REPUTATION, AUDIT, GLOBAL, EDUCATION tokens), each justified by a specific empirical coefficient, plus an activity-to-token reward matrix.
- It provides a hybrid blockchain platform recommendation: Ethereum mainnet for high-stakes/infrequent governance, Layer-2 rollups for low-cost real-time auditing, and Polkadot XCMP/cross-chain bridges for cross-border coordination, with Solidity recommended given mature auditing tooling.
- It argues the knowledge paradox and 66.6% mean knowledge-test accuracy are likely universal, so the framework generalizes beyond China and beyond media to adjacent creative industries (music via Suno/Udio, visual art via Midjourney/DALL-E/Stable Diffusion, game development).
- It explicitly positions DAO governance as a complement to, not a replacement for, statutory regulation (which is still needed for criminal enforcement, cross-border disputes, consumer/privacy protection like GDPR/CCPA, and systemic risks).

## Conclusion

The study concludes that its O-S-O-R-based behavioral model robustly explains Sora adoption intention (R² = 58%), with most hypotheses supported: interest in technology drives information elaboration, which feeds confidence, benefit, and risk perceptions, and confidence plus benefit perception drive adoption — while risk perception does not. The headline finding, the knowledge paradox, is statistically supported and interpreted as evidence that centralized information environments can mislead even well-informed professionals by amplifying their skepticism; an illustrative comparison (Figure 3) argues DAO governance would reverse this, raising high-knowledge professionals' confidence from ~0.30 under traditional systems to ~0.90 under DAOs. The authors translate these findings into the five-mechanism blockchain governance blueprint and claim it transforms expertise from a barrier into support for properly designed oversight. The paper is candid about limitations: the cross-sectional design prevents causal inference; the single-country (China) sample limits generalizability given distinct regulatory and platform ecosystems; construct operationalizations need cross-setting validation; and the DAO architecture itself is a proposed design, not an implemented or empirically tested system (and inherits known DAO problems — plutocracy, low participation, Sybil/capture risk, oracle problem, gas costs). It outlines four future-research directions: (1) longitudinal tracking of the knowledge paradox around AI policy/model releases; (2) multi-country cross-cultural replication; (3) field pilots of DAO modules with pre-registered outcomes (audit accuracy, time-to-correction, participation sustainability); and (4) economic analyses comparing token incentive efficacy versus traditional quality-assurance approaches to assess cost-effectiveness and long-run viability. The overarching open question is whether decentralized governance can durably institutionalize adaptive coordination/transparency or whether organizations revert to centralized patterns once adoption pressures ease.
