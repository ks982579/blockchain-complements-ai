## Overview

This paper is a literature review (published in *Electronics*, 2025, by Choi and Kim of Kyonggi University) that surveys research on the technological convergence of blockchain and artificial intelligence (AI), arguing that prior surveys have been too narrow because they treat one technology as merely augmenting the other (often reducing blockchain to a "data repository" role) or focus on single application domains. To address this gap, the paper systematically reviews academic papers (2019-2024, sourced via IEEE Xplore, SpringerLink, ScienceDirect, ACM Digital Library, and Google Scholar) plus industry white papers and reports (IBM, Microsoft, Google, Gartner, McKinsey), organizing them into two directions: AI applied to blockchain (categorized by blockchain layers L0/protocol, L1/data, L2/application) and blockchain applied to AI (categorized into four classes: federated learning, model learning, data sharing, and AI governance). From this analysis, the paper derives a set of cross-cutting technical challenges (trusted oracles, scalability, data privacy, consensus-AI integration, interoperability, data storage inefficiency, model versioning, regulation/standards) and proposes a "Convergence Framework" — a blockchain-protocol-based AI framework centered on a Proof-of-Useful-Work-style consensus mechanism that integrates federated learning contribution data into the consensus process, supplemented by a mainchain/sidechain multi-chain architecture for scalable AI data management. The paper's stated contribution is to provide a comprehensive, bidirectional, and balanced taxonomy of blockchain-AI convergence research together with a concrete proposed framework and future research agenda, positioned as going beyond prior reviews that focused on narrow applications (e.g., supply chain).

## Background

The paper grounds its description of blockchain fundamentals in Nakamoto's Bitcoin whitepaper (citing the original proof-of-work design and the 51% attack threshold for immutability), general consensus protocol surveys (Castor 2017), and foundational texts such as Narayanan et al.'s *Bitcoin and Cryptocurrency Technologies* and Yli-Huumo et al.'s systematic review of blockchain research. It draws on smart contract literature (Khan et al. 2021) and describes specific platforms — Ethereum and its EIP-1559 "London Upgrade" (Buterin et al. 2019), Hyperledger Fabric (Androulaki et al. 2018) with its membership service provider/ordering service architecture, and Hyperledger Besu — as representative public, permissioned, and enterprise-Ethereum-compatible blockchain designs. On the AI side, it relies on standard AI/ML framing from Russell and Norvig's *Artificial Intelligence: A Modern Approach*, federated learning surveys (Li et al. 2020), and anomaly detection surveys (Chalapathy and Chawla 2019), as well as Salah et al.'s (2019) "Blockchain for AI" review, which the paper treats as an important prior framing of open research challenges. The technical-challenges section builds on cited prior proposals for scalability solutions (Plasma, state channels, Layer-2 rollups), privacy-preserving cryptography (zero-knowledge proofs per Fiege, Fiat, and Shamir 1987; homomorphic encryption per Yi et al. 2014), interoperability protocols (Cosmos IBC, Polkadot, Chainlink), and decentralized storage systems (IPFS, Arweave, Filecoin, Storj). It also explicitly contrasts itself against a cluster of prior blockchain-AI integration reviews focused mainly on supply chain management (Tyagi et al. 2020, Ahamed and Karthikeyan 2020, Rickardo and Gladson 2023, Oriekhoe et al. 2024, Kashem et al. 2023, Emrouznejad et al. 2023, Tsolakis et al. 2023, Zawish et al. 2022), positioning these as the narrow, single-domain studies its broader, bidirectional approach is meant to surpass.

## Key Points

- The paper proposes a taxonomy splitting blockchain-AI convergence research into two directions: AI-for-blockchain (subdivided into Layer 0 protocol-level, Layer 1 data-level, and Layer 2 application-level uses of AI) and blockchain-for-AI (subdivided into Class 1 federated learning, Class 2 model learning/training, Class 3 data sharing, and Class 4 AI governance).
- Through review of Layer 0 studies, the paper claims AI is increasingly used to optimize blockchain protocol operations — e.g., converting mining power into AI compute (Nebula AI), reinforcement-learning-based optimal mining strategies (Wang et al.), and AI-driven validation of data-upload protocols and cross-chain stakeholder authentication (Alrubei et al.; Khan et al.) — and that current trends are moving beyond simple optimization toward making blockchain a foundation for AI training itself.
- The paper claims that at the data layer (Layer 1), AI techniques (anomaly detection via raw-data analysis, cross-graph analysis, SVD, clustering, collective anomaly detection) and deep-learning-based intrusion/DDoS detection are being applied to improve fraud detection, smart-contract privacy, and security on blockchain networks, citing multiple Ethereum- and Bitcoin-focused studies.
- At the application layer (Layer 2), the paper documents AI-blockchain integrations across healthcare (personalized treatment platforms, breast cancer workflow), IoT/Internet of Vehicles (secure multiparty computation), supply chain (natural gas trading via TPA-LSTM, food-supply credit assessment, adoption-prediction models), and document verification (degree-certificate verification via NLP and hashing).
- On blockchain-for-AI, the paper claims blockchain-based federated learning (BCFL) is a major research thrust addressing privacy, reward/incentive design for participant contribution, and protection against malicious updates, but notes that scalability and efficiency limitations of blockchain protocols remain a fundamental constraint on these approaches.
- The paper claims blockchain provides distinct value for AI model training/versioning (Class 2, including frameworks like Health-chain and IoHT remote patient monitoring), secure data sharing for AI (Class 3, especially in healthcare and supply chain), and AI governance/MLOps/ethical compliance (Class 4, citing blockchain-based MLOps pipelines).
- The paper identifies and characterizes eight specific technical challenges to blockchain-AI convergence: (1) trusted oracle problems (external data integrity for smart contracts and AI training), (2) scalability mismatches between blockchain throughput/storage and AI compute/data demands, (3) data privacy tensions arising from blockchain immutability versus sensitive AI training data, (4) risks and opportunities in combining AI with consensus algorithm design (including a proposed research direction toward Proof of Useful Work, PoUW), (5) interoperability across heterogeneous blockchain platforms and AI systems, (6) inefficiency of on-chain data storage for AI workloads, (7) model update/versioning conflicts with blockchain immutability, and (8) the need for regulatory frameworks and standardization.
- The paper's central original proposal is a "Convergence Framework": a blockchain-protocol-centered AI framework that (a) develops energy-efficient consensus algorithms by reprocessing AI training computation into the consensus process, (b) records AI model update weights/versioning on-chain or via sidechains, (c) enhances data privacy through cryptographic techniques balanced against blockchain transparency, and (d) is specifically designed to be applied to federated learning platforms by integrating learning-contribution evaluation into the consensus mechanism.
- As part of this framework, the paper proposes a multi-chain (mainchain/sidechain) architecture in which the mainchain manages global model updates while sidechains distribute large-scale data load, intended to improve scalability and reliability of large-scale AI data processing on blockchain.
- The paper claims its comparative positioning (Table 3) against prior blockchain-AI integration studies — most of which are domain-specific (supply chain, agriculture, business operations) — demonstrates that its systematic, bidirectional integration analysis and proposed framework offer broader scope and a clearer future research direction than existing literature.

## Conclusion

The paper concludes that the convergence of blockchain and AI represents a major technological turning point with potential for innovations achievable by neither technology alone, and that its comprehensive bidirectional review and proposed Convergence Framework fill a gap left by prior narrowly-scoped studies. However, the paper is explicit that its framework remains conceptual/proposed rather than empirically validated: it states that future research must focus on validating the practical feasibility of the proposed framework, including experimental studies on performance improvement and resource utilization, cryptographic techniques for enhanced data privacy, and verification of multi-chain structure stability for large-scale data processing. The paper acknowledges persistent open challenges — data protocol constraints (scalability, storage inefficiency), trusted oracle reliability, consensus algorithm safety when modified by AI, cross-platform interoperability, AI model version management under blockchain immutability, and the absence of mature regulatory/standardization frameworks — as unresolved areas requiring further work. It frames blockchain-based federated learning as a particularly promising but currently limited area (constrained by TPS, block capacity, and privacy/reliability issues from centralized-server federated learning) that its proposed PoUW-style consensus integration is intended to address, but this too is presented as a research direction rather than a demonstrated solution. Overall, the paper's claims are supported primarily through synthesis and categorization of existing literature rather than new experiments, and it explicitly positions itself as a roadmap-setting contribution whose proposed framework awaits future empirical validation across industrial settings.
