## Overview

This Systematization of Knowledge (Mao et al., Nanjing University et al., 2026) develops a unified, cross-layer security framework for fully autonomous LLM agents (Level 2 "delegated" and Level 3 "fully autonomous") operating in agentic commerce and finance — machine actors that hold digital wallets or delegated payment credentials, negotiate, and execute irreversible on-chain and off-chain transactions without per-transaction human approval (exemplified by OpenClaw, formerly Clawdbot). The paper's core problem is that security research is fragmented across five disconnected communities (LLM security, blockchain/DeFi security, FinTech, multi-agent systems, and mechanism design), none of which captures the full stack of an autonomous financial agent that is simultaneously an LLM, a payment-network actor, a regulated intermediary, and a multi-agent participant. Grounding its synthesis in a PRISMA-style curated public corpus (1,373 records reduced to a 142-work corpus spanning 1994–2026, with inter-coder agreement κ ≈ 0.83–0.87 on layer labeling), the SoK organizes threats along FIVE dimensions, derives TWELVE cross-layer attack vectors showing how reasoning/tooling-layer failures propagate into custody, settlement, market harm, and compliance exposure, assesses emerging agent-payment protocols (ERC-8004, AP2, OKX APP, x402, ACP/ERC-8183, MPP), and proposes a five-layer defense-in-depth architecture. Its central thesis is that securing agentic commerce is inherently a cross-layer problem requiring coordinated controls across LLM safety, protocol design, identity, market structure, and regulation, and it closes with a research roadmap and benchmark agenda.

## Background

The paper builds on and cites a wide body of prior work to support its argument. From LLM security it draws on indirect prompt injection research (Greshake et al. 2023), automatic/universal prompt injection (Liu et al. 2024), and self-replicating "Prompt Infection" propagation through multi-agent networks (Lee and Tiwari 2024), as well as memory-poisoning and poisoned-experience-retrieval attacks on Web3 agents (Patlan et al. 2025; "MemoryGraft"; Sunil et al. 2026). For autonomous financial agents it cites LLM-powered crypto portfolio management (Luo et al. 2025), agent-based finance reviews (Rizinski and Trajanov 2026), and the OpenClaw lifecycle security analysis "Taming OpenClaw" (Deng et al. 2026). It situates agent-payment protocols against prior academic security analyses — Acharya's (2025) intent/authenticity verification for trustless agent payments, MCP specification security analyses (Maloyan and Namiot 2026; Zhang et al. 2026), and protocol threat-modeling comparisons (Nieper-Wisskirchen et al. 2026). For authorization it cites foundational capability-oriented delegation work (macaroons, object-capability models) plus newer semantic task-to-scope matching and Agentic JWT delegation. For inter-agent trust it draws on Hu and Rong's (2025) brief/claim/proof/stake/reputation/constraint trust taxonomy and cryptocurrency-wallet SoK work (Erinle et al. 2025). It cites DeFi-native MEV mitigations (Flashbots Protect, MEV Blocker, orderflow auctions) for sandwich-attack defense, and runtime-verification systems ZTRV (Lan et al. 2026) and Agent-Sentry (Sequeira et al. 2026) plus deterministic pre-action authorization fabrics (ACP-Control, OAP). On regulation it grounds compliance claims in concrete legal instruments: FinCEN BSA/MSB rules (CTR >$10,000, SAR ≥$5,000), the EU MiCA Regulation (2023/1114), and FATF 2023 VASP guidance. It also revives decades of pre-LLM agent-mediated e-commerce literature (Guttman/Maes, Sandholm, Sierra) on negotiation, contract-net coordination, reputation, and the predictability-vs-flexibility trade-off, and positions itself against general LLM-agent security surveys as narrower but operationally deeper on financial irreversibility, custody, and settlement.

## Key Points

- The paper proposes a five-dimensional threat taxonomy for autonomous financial agents: D1 Agent Integrity (model poisoning, prompt injection, tool/plugin compromise, memory injection), D2 Transaction Authorization (key/credential management, spending bounds, intent verification), D3 Inter-Agent Trust (identity spoofing/Sybil, negotiation integrity, collusion, cascading prompt infection), D4 Market Manipulation (agent-driven manipulation, adversarial herding, sandwich attacks), and D5 Regulatory Compliance (KYC/AML for non-human actors, VASP classification/liability, audit trails).
- It derives and characterizes 12 cross-layer attack vectors, each with a layer path and core mechanism: P2T (Prompt-to-Transaction), T2R (Tool-to-Reasoning), A2M (Agent-to-Market), R2I (Reg-to-Integrity), T2T (Tool-to-Transaction), P2K (Prompt-to-Key), M2A (Model-to-Authorization), C2E (Collusion-to-Escrow), O2P (Oracle-to-Position), I2M (Identity-to-Market), N2C (Neg-to-Compliance), and S2I (Supply-to-Integrity).
- It argues the most dangerous attacks are cross-layer: a vulnerability at one layer (e.g., prompt injection in reasoning) cascades into harm at another (e.g., unauthorized signed token transfers in custody) while bypassing that layer's own defenses; point defenses are therefore insufficient.
- It ranks deployment risk among the vectors: P2T, T2R, T2T, and S2I are the most immediate (they convert public inputs, tools, or dependencies into directly authorized financial actions), while C2E, O2P, and N2C are slower-burn but harder to detect because harm accumulates; R2I and M2A are flagged as more speculative early-warning categories with thin corpus support.
- It distinguishes operationally similar vectors: T2R (corrupts reasoning via false data) vs. T2T (corrupts execution after correct reasoning); T2R (acute, transaction-local) vs. O2P (chronic, cumulative oracle drift); and P2T (induces a new unauthorized action) vs. P2K (coerces signing itself, requiring hard cognition/custody separation).
- It provides a protocol security assessment showing no single agent-payment protocol or execution interface covers all five dimensions: ERC-8004 (on-chain spending caps/guardrails), AP2 (payment-intent verification), OKX APP (challenge/credential auth, A2A escrow/dispute, four intents: charge/escrow/session/upto), x402 (HTTP-402 per-request auth), MPP (digest-bound auth, session escrow, receipts), ACP/ERC-8183 (Job state machine, three-role Client/Provider/Evaluator trust model, evaluator agents), and MCP (tool sandboxing/permissions) are complementary but individually insufficient.
- It introduces explicit threat modeling for the evaluator-agent trust primitive in ACP/ERC-8183, identifying four evaluator attack scenarios (direct bribery, Sybil evaluator clusters, evaluator–provider collusion, adversarial evaluator substitution via front-running) and mitigations (bonded/slashable evaluators, BFT committee sizing with VRF selection, TEE attestation, anomaly monitoring) — the basis of the C2E vector.
- It extends the agent-integrity taxonomy with three memory-specific attack sub-types (write-path poisoning, retrieval manipulation, memory-staleness exploitation) and corresponding defenses (cryptographic provenance tags on memory writes, memory verifiers, TTL-bounded entries, periodic audits).
- It proposes a five-layer defense-in-depth architecture mapped to the taxonomy: Layer 1 Prompt and Tool Hygiene (input sanitization, agent-content tagging, pre-invocation tool-provenance verification); Layer 2 Verified Execution Context (output validation, runtime context binding, capability graphs); Layer 3 Payment Authorization and Custody (cognition/custody separation, scoped spending policies, end-to-end intent binding via ERC-8004/AP2/OKX APP session keys/x402-MPP request binding); Layer 4 Inter-Agent Trust Controls (authenticated identity, stake/reputation-backed evaluator selection, collusion/Sybil anomaly monitoring); and Layer 5 Market and Compliance Monitoring (circuit breakers, cumulative position-drift detection, exposure aggregation, tamper-evident audit trails).
- It maps regulatory obligations to engineering controls, proposing a three-tier principal liability model (deployer completes KYC bound to the agent's DID; infrastructure operator is the liable VASP maintaining AML monitoring; agent instance is an automated process, not itself a regulated entity), arguing the infrastructure operator rather than the agent instance bears VASP obligations under FATF guidance.
- It argues that adversarial herding cannot be prevented by per-agent authorization controls — each agent's action may be individually authorized while the aggregate is harmful — so mitigation must operate at market/regulatory levels (portfolio circuit breakers, model-diversity mandates for large agent fleets).

## Conclusion

The paper concludes that autonomous financial-agent security is fundamentally a cross-layer problem: threats originate in reasoning, tools, identity, or inter-agent interaction, but resulting harm surfaces in custody, settlement, markets, or compliance, and the corpus synthesis confirms that point defenses are inadequate. It finds that agentic-finance risk is not the sum of traditional financial security and generic LLM security but emerges from their interaction under financial irreversibility and reduced human oversight; that no existing system or protocol offers end-to-end coverage of the threat surface (motivating the layered architecture); and that common assumptions — "a human can intervene," "prompt injection is a localized bug," "on-chain finality implies correctness" — do not hold for autonomous agents. It highlights an underappreciated systemic risk: when many agents share the same foundation model or execution stack, a single exploit can propagate into market-wide disruption. The authors are explicit about limitations: several vectors (notably R2I, M2A, C2E) have thin direct-support evidence and are marked speculative; protocol maturity varies widely (MPP most operationally mature with a live Tempo mainnet deployment; ERC-8004/ERC-8183/AP2 largely draft or research-stage); and claims about proposed protocol features are explicitly distinguished from deployed behavior. The research agenda identifies four open priorities: (1) finance-specific benchmarks that jointly capture irreversible execution, inter-agent settlement, and cumulative manipulation (current testbeds like AgentDojo, ASB, TraderBench, CAIA fall short); (2) long-horizon monitoring metrics operating over days/weeks for cumulative O2P, N2C, and correlated-agent failures; (3) a mature inter-agent trust governance layer for evaluator selection, cryptographic identity, and anti-collusion enforcement; and (4) underexplored traditional payment rails and cross-chain deployments with different reversibility, custody, and compliance assumptions. It also notes structurally hard trade-offs (latency vs. authorization strictness, portfolio vs. spot-trading tolerance) that adaptive policy tuning can only partially reconcile, leaving hard custody boundaries and market-level monitoring as irreducible requirements.
