## Overview

This paper investigates how blockchain technology and artificial intelligence (AI) adoption influence audit quality among firms in Turkey, addressing a gap identified in prior literature where blockchain has typically been studied for its effect on accounting processes and AI for its effect on audit processes, but rarely both examined together against audit quality as the outcome variable. The authors collected primary survey data from 300 respondents across 100 firms in Turkey (three respondents per firm, drawn from top, middle, and lower management levels) using random sampling across sectors, and analyzed the data using Partial Least Squares Structural Equation Modeling (PLS-SEM). Blockchain was measured across eight dimensions (technical, economic, social/trust, governance/regulatory, environmental, user experience, legal), AI across thirteen dimensions (technical, ethical, legal, human-AI collaboration, security/privacy, environmental impact, employment effects), and audit quality across nine dimensions (auditor independence, professional skepticism, competency, due care, ethical behavior, transparency, firm size, regulatory compliance, reporting accuracy), all measured on 5-point Likert scales. The study finds that both blockchain and AI utilization have statistically significant positive effects on audit quality, with AI showing a stronger effect (path coefficient 0.465) than blockchain (0.325), both significant at the 1% level. The paper contributes evidence-based insights for auditors, investors, regulators, and policymakers in Turkey on integrating these technologies into audit practice, framed within agency theory, the Technology Acceptance Model (TAM), diffusion of innovations theory, control theory, and institutional theory.

## Background

The paper situates its work within several theoretical frameworks drawn from prior literature: agency theory (relationship between principals and agents, and how technology reduces information asymmetry and agency conflicts), the Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) (defined via Hamza et al., with the link between TAM characteristics such as perceived ease of use/usefulness and auditors' technology adoption and audit quality improvements drawn from Afsay, Tahriri & Rezaee; Rosli, Yeow & Siew; and Al-Ateeq et al.), diffusion of innovations theory (Jillbert et al., on how technologies spread through organizations and ecosystems), control theory (on automated checks, real-time monitoring, and data integrity verification strengthening control environments), and institutional theory (on how regulatory bodies, professional norms, and institutional pressures shape technology adoption in auditing). It draws heavily on prior empirical and conceptual work establishing that blockchain provides a decentralized, immutable ledger enhancing data integrity, enabling continuous/real-time auditing, strengthening audit trails, and supporting smart-contract-based automation (citing Lee & Zhang, Qadir & Arab, Bonson & Bednarova, Fotoh & Lorentzon, and others), while also noting documented challenges such as scalability, regulatory uncertainty, privacy issues, and technical complexity (citing Vilkov & Tian, Mashayekhi et al.). It also builds on prior AI-and-audit literature (notably Noordin, Hussainey & Hayek; Han et al.'s literature review on blockchain and AI in accounting/auditing; Fedyk et al. on whether AI improves the audit process) describing AI's role in data analytics, automation of routine audit tasks, continuous monitoring, fraud pattern detection, and decision-support systems. The measurement instruments for blockchain dimensions are adapted from Wang (2023) and Sujanto et al. (2021); AI dimensions from Noordin et al. (2022) and Commerford et al. (2022); and audit quality dimensions from Boskou, Kirkos & Spathis (2019).

## Key Points

- AI utilization in firms' financial systems has a statistically significant positive effect on audit quality (path coefficient = 0.465, t = 8.472, p < 0.001), the strongest of the two predictors studied.
- Blockchain technology utilization has a statistically significant positive effect on audit quality (path coefficient = 0.325, t = 5.456, p < 0.001).
- Both the hypothesis that blockchain positively impacts audit quality via auditorial assistance during the audit process, and the hypothesis that AI positively impacts audit quality via support during the audit process, are supported by the empirical results.
- The reliability and validity tests (Cronbach's alpha > 0.90 for all three constructs, composite reliability > 0.90, AVE > 0.50, Fornell-Larcker discriminant validity criteria met, HTMT ratios below thresholds, and VIF values indicating no problematic multicollinearity) establish that the measurement model is methodologically sound for the PLS-SEM analysis.
- Correlation analysis shows a 66.2% correlation between AI and audit quality and a 60.6% correlation between blockchain and AI, with all inter-construct correlations below 80%, indicating low risk of multicollinearity bias.
- The model fit statistics (SRMR = 0.058, NFI = 0.837) indicate an acceptable overall structural model fit for the Turkish sample.
- Blockchain's transparency, immutability, automation via smart contracts, and real-time monitoring capabilities collectively enhance audit efficiency, accuracy, and effectiveness, consistent with and extending prior findings (e.g., Han et al., Kroon et al.) to the Turkish context specifically.
- AI improves audit quality primarily through large-scale data analysis, identification of previously unknown fraud patterns via machine learning, and real-time anomaly/fraud detection, mirroring findings from Noordin, Hussainey & Hayek and Fedyk et al. but newly demonstrated within a Turkish firm sample.
- Blockchain and AI adoption in firms' financial systems builds confidence among investors, stakeholders, and legislators by improving the reliability of financial reporting and fraud detection.
- The study provides practical implications for multiple stakeholder groups: investors can use audit-quality signals to inform investment decisions; governments/policymakers can use the findings to improve governance and regulatory mechanisms; and audit firms can use the findings to guide technology adoption strategies, training, and maintenance of auditor independence and professional skepticism during AI/blockchain integration.

## Conclusion

The study's two central hypotheses were both supported: blockchain technology and AI utilization each have a significant positive influence on audit quality in the Turkish context, with AI exerting a somewhat larger effect than blockchain. The authors interpret this as evidence that these technologies assist the audit process, support timely fraud detection, and improve the reliability of financial reporting, thereby strengthening confidence among investors, stakeholders, and regulators. The paper frames its contribution primarily as extending prior conceptual and theoretical work (TAM, agency theory, institutional theory, etc.) into a new empirical/geographic setting (Turkey) using PLS-SEM on primary survey data, rather than proposing new theory. The authors explicitly note limitations: the study covers only a single economy (Turkey), and the blockchain construct was operationalized with a limited set of dimensions/units that the authors suggest should be expanded in future work. They call for future research to (1) examine blockchain's efficiency specifically within accounting systems and financial-statement preparation processes, (2) incorporate additional blockchain dimensions/units into measurement models, and (3) conduct cross-country comparative studies to assess how the use and efficiency of the artificial intelligence system vary across different economies. No negative or null findings are reported for either technology's relationship with audit quality, though the paper does acknowledge (via cited literature) that other studies have found blockchain can sometimes reduce audit quality due to misuse or unskilled personnel — a tension the present study's positive findings do not directly reconcile.
