## Background

This paper situates itself within a broad body of prior literature on blockchain fundamentals, blockchain optimization techniques, and emerging AI/blockchain integration research. The following are the key strands of prior work and external claims the paper draws on.

**Origins and foundational properties of blockchain.** The paper traces blockchain back to its original proposal in 2008 as the underlying framework for Bitcoin (Nakamoto, ref. [125]), and cites other sources for blockchain's core properties — decentralization, tamper-resistance, transparency, traceability, and immutability ([151, 161, 162], [113, 134]) — as the basis for its appeal across sectors such as healthcare, supply chain, finance, voting, identity management, and insurance.

**Domain applications cited as context.** The paper cites a number of existing systems and prior studies as examples of blockchain's applied potential, which a researcher may want to investigate further: MedChain [58] for patient-controlled medical records; smart-contract-based healthcare efficiency claims [135]; blockchain's role in addressing delays in traditional stock market intermediation [96, 175]; blockchain-based voting trust [1]; insurance smart contracts [74]; identity management systems such as SelfKey [155] and related work [16, 67, 124, 203]; supply chain traceability solutions [133, 154, 183, 185]; and newer application areas including the metaverse (Decentraland [97]), NFTs (CryptoPunks [205], [189]), and decentralized identity.

**Methodology for the systematic review.** The paper's review methodology (planning, conducting, reporting phases) follows the systematic literature review guidelines of Budgen and Brereton [39]. It also positions itself against a set of prior surveys on blockchain and AI integration ([6, 72, 80, 100, 121, 146, 152, 196, 207]), which it characterizes as having focused on AI/blockchain applications in IoT, AIoT, edge computing, decentralized intelligence, and general advantages of blockchain (transparency, security) without specifically addressing how AI can resolve blockchain's core technical limitations — a gap this paper claims to fill.

**Blockchain limitations: security.** The paper's taxonomy of blockchain attacks and vulnerabilities draws on prior definitions and categorizations: vulnerability and attack definitions from [130] and [52]; double-spending attack literature [87, 148]; mining pool attack analysis [49]; wallet/key-generation vulnerabilities from cryptographic literature [36, 159]; blockchain P2P network attack characterizations [197]; and smart contract attack/exploit taxonomies [12] (with further detail referenced in the paper's Table 11).

**Blockchain limitations: scalability and performance.** The discussion of scalability bottlenecks builds on prior work defining and measuring consensus mechanisms [206, 95], transaction throughput [123], confirmation latency [129], block size [76], network load and transaction pooling [60], node configuration effects [149], network size effects [111], transaction cost dynamics [15], and smart contract execution complexity/latency [78].

**Prior optimization solutions surveyed.** A large portion of the paper's background consists of existing (non-AI) blockchain scaling solutions that it analyzes and categorizes, which represent significant prior engineering work the researcher may want to explore: Rollups (Truebit [179], Arbitrum [86]); Payment Channels (Lightning Network [84], Raiden Network [141], Sprites [117], Duplex Micropayment Channels [59]); Sidechains (Plasma [85], Pegged sidechains [17], Liquidity Network [99]); Cross-chain systems (Cosmos [51] with Tendermint consensus [63], Polkadot [40]); Sharding approaches (Elastico [107], RsCoin [56], OmniLedger [90], QuarkChain [139], Monoxide [188], RapidChain [200], Ostraka [109], and others including Zilliqa, Elrond, Harmony, Ethereum 2.0); Block data reduction techniques (SegWit [104], Bitcoin Cash hard fork [41, 42], data aggregation approach by Danzi et al. [57]); and a wide range of alternative consensus protocols (PoS [127], PoA [25], PoB [88], PoSpace/PoC [68, 115], PoI/PoWeight [8], PoSearch [153], optimized PPoV [194], GBT-CHAIN [187], B-PDA [14], and edge-computing-based mining frameworks by Liu et al. [101] and Casado-Vara et al. [41]).

**AI/ML foundations cited.** For grounding its discussion of AI, the paper cites general AI subfield taxonomies [91] and Russell and Norvig's foundational textbook treatment of intelligent agents [145], along with the Belief-Desire-Intention (BDI) agent model [61] and broader claims about intelligent agents' societal impact [201, 7].

**Prior AI-driven blockchain solutions reviewed.** The paper's core evidence base for AI/blockchain integration consists of roughly 30 prior studies it selected via its inclusion/exclusion criteria, including: learning-based consensus protocols such as WekaCoin (Proof-of-Learning) [37], PoLe [94], Proof of Deep Learning (PoDL) [47], S-PoDL [106], Coin.AI (Proof-of-Useful-Work) [20], PoDLwHO [118], BlockML [116], and Proof of Human work [32] (with reference to CAPTCHA [33] and IPFS [24] for model storage); scalability solutions using federated and deep reinforcement learning such as Proof of Federated Learning [138], SkyChain [202], DRL-based IIoT optimization [102], DQN-based shard tuning [199], and DRL-enabled adaptive consensus selection [137]; security/anomaly-detection approaches including an encoder-decoder anomaly detector [150], PeerClassifier [173], and related works [35, 73, 62], fraud-account detection via temporal blockchain structure [3], T-S fuzzy ANN traceability [44], collaborative clustering for intrusion detection [98], XGBoost-based Ponzi scheme detection [46], and supervised-ML pre-consensus filtering on Hyperledger Fabric [147]; efficiency-focused node-selection methods such as Proof of Artificial Intelligence (PoAI) [43], machine-learning-based PoW variants [105], DAIBCN [92], reinforcement-learning mining selection [190], edge-computing mining offload with neural-network-based auctions (citing ECSP concepts [71]) [128], and ML-based fork prediction [120]; and governance/dependency-reduction work such as NeuroChain [48]. The paper also references general claims about privacy-preserving blockchain techniques — zero-knowledge proofs (Zcash) [23], homomorphic computation [204], multiparty computation [9], and privacy-preserving smart contracts [22, 182, 181] — and a broader claim that AI/ML can serve as a surrogate for blockchain optimization processes without degrading performance [38].

These cited works collectively form the evidentiary and conceptual foundation the paper builds on to argue that AI and ML can address blockchain's persistent sustainability, scalability, security, efficiency, and governance limitations, and to identify directions for future AI-driven blockchain research.
