## Conclusion

The survey set out to answer six research questions covering blockchain's emerging applications, its core adoption barriers (security and scalability/performance), the state-of-the-art non-AI optimization techniques (rollups, payment channels, multi-chain/sharding, block-data compression, and consensus protocol redesigns), and—most centrally—how AI and ML can be integrated to address blockchain's remaining limitations. The authors conclude that their core premise is supported: across the 30 AI/ML-focused papers they reviewed, AI and ML techniques (deep learning, reinforcement learning, federated learning, decision trees, neural-network-based auctions, etc.) have already been applied with concrete benefits to several of blockchain's open problems, particularly around energy consumption/sustainability and security. The review finds that AI-enhanced consensus mechanisms (e.g., Proof of Federated Learning, Proof of Deep Learning, Proof of Artificial Intelligence) can redirect the computational energy normally wasted on arbitrary hash puzzles toward useful machine-learning tasks, and that AI-based anomaly detection, intrusion detection, and fraud/Ponzi-scheme classification methods can materially improve blockchain security and privacy.

At the same time, the paper is explicit that AI-blockchain integration is still in its infancy and that several of its own research questions (especially RQ6, on unresolved issues) remain only partially answered. It identifies a number of specific limitations and gaps that are framed as open research directions:

- **Security solutions are reactive, not predictive.** Existing AI-based detection methods (anomaly detection, intrusion detection, behavior classification) operate after the fact and are too slow to stop an attack in real time. The authors note there is currently no system that can simulate attacks proactively or identify compromised identities/security flaws as they emerge—an explicit call for predictive, real-time AI security solutions.
- **Efficiency-focused AI consensus solutions trade off decentralization or energy use.** Node-selection approaches (e.g., POAI, PoKW, DAIBCN) improve speed and resource use but tend to centralize trust around "super nodes" or whitelists, undermining blockchain's egalitarian design; conversely, reinforcement-learning-based mining approaches (e.g., [190], [128]) improve throughput but still rely on energy-intensive PoW at their core. The paper frames reconciling AI-driven efficiency gains with decentralization and sustainability as unresolved.
- **Blockchain governance and "smart" smart contracts.** Current smart contracts are described as not actually "smart"—limited to simple, predefined logic. The authors point to a future direction where AI embedded in smart contracts could enable autonomous, tamper-proof, and transparent arbitration (e.g., automated mortgages, escrow, tax processing, counterfeit-proof digital identity), effectively creating an AI-governed blockchain.
- **Scalability remains an open and growing problem.** Despite techniques like AI-based block pruning, the authors state that current scalability solutions are struggling to keep pace with blockchain adoption growth, and call for combining AI techniques with existing scaling approaches (sharding, rollups, multi-chain) to meet future demand.
- **The "data gatekeeper" problem.** As blockchain-based open data becomes more widespread, the paper identifies a new challenge in validating, aggregating, and interpreting external data sources for use in on-chain decision-making—an area it suggests AI is well-suited to address but that has not yet been substantively tackled.
- **Standardization and normalization of AI-blockchain convergence.** More broadly, the conclusion flags consensus-protocol design, standardization, and normalization of AI-blockchain integration as unresolved problems with "tremendous potential" for future research.

From a managerial/practical standpoint, the authors argue that despite blockchain's appeal (decentralization, transparency, immutability), its scalability, performance, security, and energy limitations still impede mainstream business adoption, and that AI/ML integration—particularly as a "surrogate" for optimization processes without degrading blockchain performance—offers a promising but still maturing path forward. Overall, the paper's central claim that AI can meaningfully complement and improve blockchain is supported by the evidence it surveys, but the authors are careful to frame this as an early-stage research area with substantial unresolved technical and governance challenges, positioning their taxonomy of gaps as a roadmap for future AI-driven blockchain research.
