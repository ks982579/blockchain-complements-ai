## Overview

This paper is a systematic literature review (ACM Computing Surveys, 2024) examining how artificial intelligence and machine learning can address blockchain's persistent adoption barriers — namely security vulnerabilities, scalability/performance bottlenecks, sustainability (energy consumption), efficiency, and governance/dependency issues. Following a structured methodology (search strategy, inclusion/exclusion criteria, and systematic filtering from an initial 158 documents down to 74 relevant papers and ultimately 30 focused on AI/ML-based blockchain solutions), the authors frame their analysis around six research questions covering blockchain's emerging applications (e.g., metaverse, NFTs, decentralized identity), its layered architecture and attack taxonomy (network, consensus, data, execution, application layers; double-spending, mining-pool, wallet, network, and smart-contract attacks), and existing non-AI scalability solutions (rollups, payment channels, sidechains, cross-chains, sharding, block-data compression, and alternative consensus protocols like PoS, PoA, PoB, PoSpace). The core contribution is a detailed taxonomy and comparative analysis of 30 AI-driven solutions organized by the challenge they address — sustainability (e.g., WekaCoin, PoLe, PoDL, S-PoDL, Coin.AI, BlockML, which replace wasteful PoW hashing with useful ML/DL model training as consensus proofs), scalability (e.g., PoFL via federated learning, SkyChain and DRL/DQN-based dynamic sharding frameworks that tune shard count, block size, and resharding intervals in real time), and security (deep learning encoder-decoder anomaly detection, fraud/Ponzi-scheme detection via supervised/unsupervised ML, intrusion detection via clustering-based data fusion). The survey distinguishes itself from prior reviews by focusing specifically on AI as a remediation mechanism for blockchain's core technical limitations rather than on general blockchain-AI integration for IoT or other application domains, and it concludes by identifying under-explored research directions — particularly in efficiency and governance/dependency — where AI-driven optimization of blockchain remains immature, positioning the work as a roadmap for future research at the blockchain-AI intersection.

## Background

This paper situates itself within a broad body of prior literature on blockchain fundamentals, blockchain optimization techniques, and emerging AI/blockchain integration research. The following are the key strands of prior work and external claims the paper draws on.

**Origins and foundational properties of blockchain.** The paper traces blockchain back to its original proposal in 2008 as the underlying framework for Bitcoin (Nakamoto, ref. [125]), and cites other sources for blockchain's core properties — decentralization, tamper-resistance, transparency, traceability, and immutability ([151, 161, 162], [113, 134]) — as the basis for its appeal across sectors such as healthcare, supply chain, finance, voting, identity management, and insurance.

**Domain applications cited as context.** The paper cites a number of existing systems and prior studies as examples of blockchain's applied potential, which a researcher may want to investigate further: MedChain [58] for patient-controlled medical records; smart-contract-based healthcare efficiency claims [135]; blockchain's role in addressing delays in traditional stock market intermediation [96, 175]; blockchain-based voting trust [1]; insurance smart contracts [74]; identity management systems such as SelfKey [155] and related work [16, 67, 124, 203]; supply chain traceability solutions [133, 154, 183, 185]; and newer application areas including the metaverse (Decentraland [97]), NFTs (CryptoPunks [205], [189]), and decentralized identity.

**Methodology for the systematic review.** The paper's review methodology (planning, conducting, reporting phases) follows the systematic literature review guidelines of Budgen and Brereton [39]. It also positions itself against a set of prior surveys on blockchain and AI integration ([6, 72, 80, 100, 121, 146, 152, 196, 207]), which it characterizes as having focused on AI/blockchain applications in IoT, AIoT, edge computing, decentralized intelligence, and general advantages of blockchain (transparency, security) without specifically addressing how AI can resolve blockchain's core technical limitations — a gap this paper claims to fill.

**Blockchain limitations: security.** The paper's taxonomy of blockchain attacks and vulnerabilities draws on prior definitions and categorizations: vulnerability and attack definitions from [130] and [52]; double-spending attack literature [87, 148]; mining pool attack analysis [49]; wallet/key-generation vulnerabilities from cryptographic literature [36, 159]; blockchain P2P network attack characterizations [197]; and smart contract attack/exploit taxonomies [12] (with further detail referenced in the paper's Table 11).

**Blockchain limitations: scalability and performance.** The discussion of scalability bottlenecks builds on prior work defining and measuring consensus mechanisms [206, 95], transaction throughput [123], confirmation latency [129], block size [76], network load and transaction pooling [60], node configuration effects [149], network size effects [111], transaction cost dynamics [15], and smart contract execution complexity/latency [78].

**Prior optimization solutions surveyed.** A large portion of the paper's background consists of existing (non-AI) blockchain scaling solutions that it analyzes and categorizes, which represent significant prior engineering work the researcher may want to explore: Rollups (Truebit [179], Arbitrum [86]); Payment Channels (Lightning Network [84], Raiden Network [141], Sprites [117], Duplex Micropayment Channels [59]); Sidechains (Plasma [85], Pegged sidechains [17], Liquidity Network [99]); Cross-chain systems (Cosmos [51] with Tendermint consensus [63], Polkadot [40]); Sharding approaches (Elastico [107], RsCoin [56], OmniLedger [90], QuarkChain [139], Monoxide [188], RapidChain [200], Ostraka [109], and others including Zilliqa, Elrond, Harmony, Ethereum 2.0); Block data reduction techniques (SegWit [104], Bitcoin Cash hard fork [41, 42], data aggregation approach by Danzi et al. [57]); and a wide range of alternative consensus protocols (PoS [127], PoA [25], PoB [88], PoSpace/PoC [68, 115], PoI/PoWeight [8], PoSearch [153], optimized PPoV [194], GBT-CHAIN [187], B-PDA [14], and edge-computing-based mining frameworks by Liu et al. [101] and Casado-Vara et al. [41]).

**AI/ML foundations cited.** For grounding its discussion of AI, the paper cites general AI subfield taxonomies [91] and Russell and Norvig's foundational textbook treatment of intelligent agents [145], along with the Belief-Desire-Intention (BDI) agent model [61] and broader claims about intelligent agents' societal impact [201, 7].

**Prior AI-driven blockchain solutions reviewed.** The paper's core evidence base for AI/blockchain integration consists of roughly 30 prior studies it selected via its inclusion/exclusion criteria, including: learning-based consensus protocols such as WekaCoin (Proof-of-Learning) [37], PoLe [94], Proof of Deep Learning (PoDL) [47], S-PoDL [106], Coin.AI (Proof-of-Useful-Work) [20], PoDLwHO [118], BlockML [116], and Proof of Human work [32] (with reference to CAPTCHA [33] and IPFS [24] for model storage); scalability solutions using federated and deep reinforcement learning such as Proof of Federated Learning [138], SkyChain [202], DRL-based IIoT optimization [102], DQN-based shard tuning [199], and DRL-enabled adaptive consensus selection [137]; security/anomaly-detection approaches including an encoder-decoder anomaly detector [150], PeerClassifier [173], and related works [35, 73, 62], fraud-account detection via temporal blockchain structure [3], T-S fuzzy ANN traceability [44], collaborative clustering for intrusion detection [98], XGBoost-based Ponzi scheme detection [46], and supervised-ML pre-consensus filtering on Hyperledger Fabric [147]; efficiency-focused node-selection methods such as Proof of Artificial Intelligence (PoAI) [43], machine-learning-based PoW variants [105], DAIBCN [92], reinforcement-learning mining selection [190], edge-computing mining offload with neural-network-based auctions (citing ECSP concepts [71]) [128], and ML-based fork prediction [120]; and governance/dependency-reduction work such as NeuroChain [48]. The paper also references general claims about privacy-preserving blockchain techniques — zero-knowledge proofs (Zcash) [23], homomorphic computation [204], multiparty computation [9], and privacy-preserving smart contracts [22, 182, 181] — and a broader claim that AI/ML can serve as a surrogate for blockchain optimization processes without degrading performance [38].

These cited works collectively form the evidentiary and conceptual foundation the paper builds on to argue that AI and ML can address blockchain's persistent sustainability, scalability, security, efficiency, and governance limitations, and to identify directions for future AI-driven blockchain research.

## Key Points

- This survey provides a systematic literature review of blockchain optimization research published from 2015–2023, narrowing 158 initial search results down to 74 papers on general blockchain optimization and 30 papers specifically on AI/ML-based blockchain optimization solutions.
- The paper organizes blockchain scalability/performance solutions into five categories—Rollups (e.g., Truebit, Arbitrum), Payment Channels (e.g., Lightning Network, Raiden Network, Sprites, Bitcoin DMC), Multi-Chain approaches (sidechains like Plasma and Pegged sidechains, cross-chain systems like Cosmos and Polkadot, and sharding solutions like Elastico, OmniLedger, RapidChain, Monoxide, QuarkChain), Block Data techniques (e.g., SegWit, Bitcoin-Cash block size increases, block data aggregation), and Consensus protocol enhancements—and summarizes the benefits and limitations of each category.
- The survey provides a structured taxonomy of blockchain attacks and vulnerabilities, categorizing them by blockchain version (1.0–3.0), architectural layer (network, consensus, data, execution, application), affected process (network discovery, transaction creation, mining, block validation), and affected actor (miner, mining pool, user, seller, exchange), and presents a detailed catalog of double-spending, mining/pool, wallet, network, and smart contract attacks (including 51% Attack, Selfish Mining, Sybil Attack, DDoS, and Reentrancy among others).
- The paper identifies and synthesizes AI/ML-based consensus mechanisms that repurpose computational effort from "wasteful" hashing into useful machine learning work, including Proof-of-Learning (WekaCoin, PoLe), Proof of Deep Learning (PoDL, S-PoDL), Proof of Useful Work (Coin.AI), PoDLwHO (deep learning with hyperparameter optimization), BlockML, and Proof of Human Work (PoH), and compares their respective model-storage strategies (on-chain, centralized server, distributed/IPFS).
- The survey demonstrates that AI/ML techniques—particularly deep reinforcement learning (e.g., SkyChain, DRL-based IIoT frameworks, DQN-based shard adjustment)—are being applied to dynamically tune blockchain scalability parameters such as shard count, block size, block intervals, and consensus protocol selection in real time.
- The paper shows that AI/ML methods (deep learning encoder-decoder anomaly detection, supervised/unsupervised fraud-account classification, T–S fuzzy ANN traceability models, clustering-based intrusion detection, and XGBoost-based Ponzi-scheme detection) are being used to enhance blockchain security through anomaly and intrusion detection, fraud detection, and behavior classification.
- The survey identifies AI-based efficiency improvements in blockchain mining, including machine-learning-based "super node" selection (Proof of Artificial Intelligence/POAI), reinforcement-learning-based leader selection, decision-tree-based miner selection with data pruning (DAIBCN), neural-network-based auction mechanisms for mobile edge computing offloading, and ML-based fork prediction (Mohammadi et al., comparing KNN, Naive Bayes, Decision Tree, and Multilayer Perceptron).
- The paper highlights NeuroChain as an example of an AI-governed, agent-based blockchain approach aimed at reducing dependency on powerful nodes and replacing complex hash-puzzle validation with an AI-driven consensus mechanism based on evaluating member interactions and exchange quality.
- The authors argue, based on their comparative analysis (Table 3), that this survey is distinct from prior blockchain-AI surveys because it focuses specifically on AI-driven solutions to resolve core blockchain limitations (sustainability, scalability, security, efficiency, governance/dependency) rather than on general applications of blockchain and AI in domains like IoT or AIoT.
- The survey concludes that energy consumption (sustainability) and security are currently the most actively researched topics for AI-blockchain integration, while efficiency and governance/dependency issues remain comparatively underexplored.
- The paper identifies unresolved research gaps where AI could be further applied, including: real-time prediction of security breaches and attack simulation (rather than reactive detection), AI-driven blockchain governance via smart contracts capable of autonomous, tamper-proof arbitration, AI-based pruning techniques for improved scalability, and "data gatekeeper" AI systems for validating and aggregating open blockchain data.
- The paper provides a consolidated reference taxonomy (glossary, abbreviations, and detailed appendix tables) covering blockchain versions, layers, processes, actors, consensus algorithms, hash functions, and performance metrics (throughput, block time, confirmation time, power consumption) for major blockchains (Bitcoin, Ethereum, Litecoin, Cardano, EOS, Ripple, and others).

## Conclusion

The survey set out to answer six research questions covering blockchain's emerging applications, its core adoption barriers (security and scalability/performance), the state-of-the-art non-AI optimization techniques (rollups, payment channels, multi-chain/sharding, block-data compression, and consensus protocol redesigns), and—most centrally—how AI and ML can be integrated to address blockchain's remaining limitations. The authors conclude that their core premise is supported: across the 30 AI/ML-focused papers they reviewed, AI and ML techniques (deep learning, reinforcement learning, federated learning, decision trees, neural-network-based auctions, etc.) have already been applied with concrete benefits to several of blockchain's open problems, particularly around energy consumption/sustainability and security. The review finds that AI-enhanced consensus mechanisms (e.g., Proof of Federated Learning, Proof of Deep Learning, Proof of Artificial Intelligence) can redirect the computational energy normally wasted on arbitrary hash puzzles toward useful machine-learning tasks, and that AI-based anomaly detection, intrusion detection, and fraud/Ponzi-scheme classification methods can materially improve blockchain security and privacy.

At the same time, the paper is explicit that AI-blockchain integration is still in its infancy and that several of its own research questions (especially RQ6, on unresolved issues) remain only partially answered. It identifies a number of specific limitations and gaps that are framed as open research directions:

- **Security solutions are reactive, not predictive.** Existing AI-based detection methods (anomaly detection, intrusion detection, behavior classification) operate after the fact and are too slow to stop an attack in real time. The authors note there is currently no system that can simulate attacks proactively or identify compromised identities/security flaws as they emerge—an explicit call for predictive, real-time AI security solutions.
- **Efficiency-focused AI consensus solutions trade off decentralization or energy use.** Node-selection approaches (e.g., POAI, PoKW, DAIBCN) improve speed and resource use but tend to centralize trust around "super nodes" or whitelists, undermining blockchain's egalitarian design; conversely, reinforcement-learning-based mining approaches (e.g., [190], [128]) improve throughput but still rely on energy-intensive PoW at their core. The paper frames reconciling AI-driven efficiency gains with decentralization and sustainability as unresolved.
- **Blockchain governance and "smart" smart contracts.** Current smart contracts are described as not actually "smart"—limited to simple, predefined logic. The authors point to a future direction where AI embedded in smart contracts could enable autonomous, tamper-proof, and transparent arbitration (e.g., automated mortgages, escrow, tax processing, counterfeit-proof digital identity), effectively creating an AI-governed blockchain.
- **Scalability remains an open and growing problem.** Despite techniques like AI-based block pruning, the authors state that current scalability solutions are struggling to keep pace with blockchain adoption growth, and call for combining AI techniques with existing scaling approaches (sharding, rollups, multi-chain) to meet future demand.
- **The "data gatekeeper" problem.** As blockchain-based open data becomes more widespread, the paper identifies a new challenge in validating, aggregating, and interpreting external data sources for use in on-chain decision-making—an area it suggests AI is well-suited to address but that has not yet been substantively tackled.
- **Standardization and normalization of AI-blockchain convergence.** More broadly, the conclusion flags consensus-protocol design, standardization, and normalization of AI-blockchain integration as unresolved problems with "tremendous potential" for future research.

From a managerial/practical standpoint, the authors argue that despite blockchain's appeal (decentralization, transparency, immutability), its scalability, performance, security, and energy limitations still impede mainstream business adoption, and that AI/ML integration—particularly as a "surrogate" for optimization processes without degrading blockchain performance—offers a promising but still maturing path forward. Overall, the paper's central claim that AI can meaningfully complement and improve blockchain is supported by the evidence it surveys, but the authors are careful to frame this as an early-stage research area with substantial unresolved technical and governance challenges, positioning their taxonomy of gaps as a roadmap for future AI-driven blockchain research.
