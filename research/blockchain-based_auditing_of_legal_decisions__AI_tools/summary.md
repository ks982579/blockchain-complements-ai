## Overview

This paper, published in *Engineering Applications of Artificial Intelligence* (2024) by Swati Sachan and Xi Liu (Lisa), addresses a pressing problem at the intersection of legal practice, explainable AI (XAI), generative AI, and blockchain: how law firms can responsibly exploit the drafting capabilities of Large Language Models (LLMs) such as GPT-4 and Google Bard while protecting client confidentiality and maintaining accountability for AI-assisted legal decisions. Rather than allowing LLMs to handle raw legal evidence directly — which risks both unreliable "black-box" reasoning and confidentiality breaches through prompt exposure — the authors propose an architecture in which an explainable, rule-based model (the Individual and Conjunctive Maximum-Likelihood Evidential Reasoning approach, I-MAKER & C-MAKER) generates structured legal decisions from case evidence, which are then anonymized and passed to Generative AI tools solely to produce human-readable textual explanations and draft correspondence. To ensure that this entire pipeline can be audited and that any tampering with XAI decisions or AI-generated content can be detected, the paper designs a blockchain-based auditing system that combines hybrid on-chain/off-chain data storage (Cloud, IPFS, and blockchain) with Merkle-root-based integrity verification. The system is implemented and evaluated through a case study on employer (vicarious) liability cases under tort law, tested across both Ethereum (public) and Hyperledger Fabric (private) blockchain platforms, and validated through a usability study with law students comparing AI-assisted versus unassisted legal drafting.

## Background

The paper builds on several interconnected research threads. In the XAI-for-legal-reasoning space, it draws on prior work applying logic-based and argumentation frameworks to legal knowledge representation, including the survey of law and logic by Prakken and Sartor (2015), the Abstract Dialectical Framework (ADF) model for predicting European Court of Human Rights judgments by Collenette et al. (2023), and earlier case-based reasoning systems such as HYPO (Rissland and Ashley, 1987), CATO (Aleven, 1997), and IBP (Bruninghaus and Ashley, 2003). It also references Bayesian approaches to legal syllogistic reasoning (Neil et al., 2019; Constant, 2023) and explainable deep-learning methods for legal text summarization using attention scores, LIME (Ribeiro et al., 2016), SHAP (Lundberg and Lee, 2017), and Layer-Wise Relevance Propagation (Binder et al., 2016). Most directly, it extends the authors' own prior work on the MAKER (Maximum-Likelihood Evidential Reasoning) framework and its I-MAKER/C-MAKER extensions for processing ambiguous categorical legal evidence under Dempster-Shafer theory (Sachan et al., 2020; Sachan et al., 2021). For the blockchain auditing component, the paper situates itself against prior data-integrity verification schemes that rely on Third-Party Auditors (TPA) — such as Provable Data Possession (Shah et al., 2008; Juels and Kaliski, 2007) and homomorphic verifiable signatures (Shacham and Waters, 2013) — which suffer from single points of failure, as well as blockchain-based alternatives such as Liu et al. (2017), Zikratov et al. (2017), Hao et al. (2020), and the Merkle-tree-based Blockchain Audit Trail (BAT) for forest fire surveillance by Datta and Sinha (2023), and the IPFS/Ethereum-based XAI decision auditing by Malhotra et al. (2021) — both of which serve as direct comparative baselines in the paper's experiments. The work also connects to the broader literature on integrating blockchain with AI systems, including Han et al.'s (2023) review of blockchain/AI in accounting and auditing, Sachan and Muwanga's (2023) blockchain-secured explainable deep neural network for medical indemnity insurance, and Sachan et al.'s (2023) blockchain framework for small-business loan decisions using fuzzy cognitive maps.

## Key Points

- The paper proposes an end-to-end architecture for a blockchain-based auditing system that detects unauthorized alterations to data repositories containing XAI-derived legal decisions and Generative AI-produced textual explanations, aiming to support responsible AI usage and accountability tracing in law firms.
- It conducts a stakeholder-driven requirement analysis (Table 2) identifying six key design objectives: compliance with data protection laws (GDPR Articles 5, 17, 22 and the CPRA), confidentiality and integrity of legal data, quality assurance via regular audits, robustness/security against adversarial attacks, support for regulatory audits, and scalability/efficiency.
- The architecture has three core components: a front-end dashboard for lawyers and legal analysts, a back-end API server (using a Proxy Re-Encryption scheme for secure data sharing between data owners/lawyers and auditors), and a hybrid on-chain/off-chain data storage mechanism.
- Off-chain storage uses Cloud (Azure Blob, storing encrypted but de-anonymized data for internal use) and IPFS via Pinata (storing encrypted and anonymized metadata), while on-chain storage (Ethereum or Hyperledger Fabric) holds only hash pointers and Merkle roots — keeping the blockchain itself free of sensitive raw data in compliance with data protection laws like the GDPR's "Right to be Forgotten."
- The blockchain network is split into two independent full nodes — a Hash Storage Node (records hashes of XAI decisions, algorithmic metadata, and Generative AI content) and an Audit Node (records the tampering status results from each audit) — to partition computational/storage workload and enhance scalability.
- Algorithm 1 details the data preparation pipeline: anonymizing the XAI decision JSON file, sending the anonymized text plus a standardized prompt to a Generative AI API, encrypting/packaging data for Cloud and IPFS, computing per-file hashes and Merkle roots (via SHA-256), encoding to BASE64, and uploading to off-chain and on-chain storage.
- Algorithm 2 defines the auditing procedure: for each legal case file, retrieve and decrypt the off-chain Cloud and IPFS data, recompute SHA-256 hashes and Merkle roots locally, and compare them against the immutable Merkle roots previously stored on-chain; a mismatch (tampering flag τ = 0) is permanently logged in the audit node with a timestamp.
- Computational complexity analysis shows I-MAKER runs in O(Q), C-MAKER in O(Q!) (the dominant cost of Algorithm 1), Merkle root computation in O(log n), and the full audit loop (Algorithm 2) in O(X·log X), where X is the number of legal case files.
- Two security lemmas are proven: (1) tampering with on-chain data requires altering all subsequent blocks across a distributed network of nodes, with the example that tampering 8 blocks after block 5 across 21 nodes would require altering ~3.97×10^10 blocks; (2) the SHA-256 hash function (256-bit, the paper's text says "64-bit" in one place but the algorithm uses SHA-256) offers negligible collision probability under birthday-attack analysis, defending against engineered hash collisions.
- The proposed I-MAKER & C-MAKER explainable model is demonstrated on a dataset of 3,585 employer liability cases (tort law, vicarious liability for workplace accidents), modeling three legal elements — duty of care, breach of duty, and causation — and producing joint probability mass distributions over possible decisions (e.g., Liability vs. No-liability), evaluated via AUC and F1 scores from 3-fold cross-validation.
- A usability study with 50 final-year law students (25 experimental using AI-generated explanations, 25 control without) found GPT-4 ("gpt-4") produced the highest-quality drafting content, with 58.5% of its output used in final letters (vs. lower scores for Bard, text-davinci-003, and text-davinci-002), and the fastest average drafting time (6 minutes vs. 8 minutes for Bard), while letter quality grades across groups remained similar (73-75 range).
- Blockchain performance experiments on Ethereum 2.0 and Hyperledger Fabric 2.0 (varying batch timeout, message count, and gas parameters) showed Hyperledger Fabric achieved higher throughput and lower latency than Ethereum, and consumed roughly seven times less memory; both networks degraded in throughput/latency beyond 12 nodes, with Hyperledger failing beyond 20 nodes.
- A comparative analysis against two baseline auditing methods — the Merkle-tree-based BAT framework (Datta and Sinha, 2023) and the IPFS/Ethereum XAI audit method (Malhotra et al., 2021) — showed the proposed approach achieved 23.65% and 21.67% higher throughput, and 16.36% and 9.38% lower audit time, respectively, attributable to batch processing and the two-node (hash storage/audit) separation of duties.

## Conclusion

The paper concludes that combining explainable AI, generative AI, and blockchain technology offers a practical pathway toward responsible and auditable use of AI in legal practice, particularly for drafting pre-litigation correspondence. By having the I-MAKER & C-MAKER evidential reasoning model generate transparent, structured decisions on tort liability, and restricting Generative AI tools to the role of producing readable textual explanations from anonymized inputs, the system mitigates both the black-box reasoning concerns associated with LLMs and the confidentiality risks of exposing sensitive case data through prompts. The blockchain-based auditing framework — built on a hybrid on-chain/off-chain storage strategy and Merkle-root comparison — was shown to detect unauthorized data tampering, comply with data protection regulations such as GDPR, and outperform two comparable prior auditing methods in throughput and audit time across both Ethereum and Hyperledger Fabric deployments. The experimental study with law students further validated that GPT-4-generated explanations could meaningfully accelerate legal drafting without compromising quality. The authors acknowledge limitations, including the operational burden of self-managed blockchain nodes (suggesting future reliance on specialized node service providers) and the inherent complexity of automating legal decisions given law's dynamic and context-dependent nature. They propose a future "Lawyer-in-the-Loop" augmentation framework, potentially incorporating knowledge distillation techniques, to enable active knowledge exchange between legal practitioners and AI systems. While the case study focuses on employer liability (tort law) cases, the authors argue the evidential reasoning and auditing methodology generalizes to other tort categories (economic, property, dignitary, strict/absolute liability, nuisance) and potentially other branches of law and other domains seeking to monitor and audit Generative AI usage.
