## Overview

This paper, published in IEEE Access in January 2019 by Salah, Rehman, Nizamuddin, and Al-Fuqaha, is a survey of how blockchain technology can be applied to artificial intelligence (AI), motivated by the observation that conventional centralized AI suffers from data tampering risks, unverifiable data provenance, and lack of trust in algorithmic decisions. The authors argue that combining blockchain's decentralized, immutable, cryptographically-secured ledger and smart-contract execution model with AI's learning and decision-making capabilities gives rise to "decentralized AI" — systems that can learn, infer, and act on trusted, shared, tamper-proof data without relying on a trusted third party. The paper's core contributions are: (1) an overview of blockchain fundamentals and how its features map onto AI needs; (2) a discussion of the decentralized-economy ecosystem enabled by AI-blockchain integration; (3) a detailed taxonomy of decentralized AI applications/operations, blockchain infrastructure types, and consensus protocols; (4) a survey of real-world and proposed use cases across domains (healthcare, energy, transportation, agriculture, supply chain, finance, real estate, robotics swarms, ocean exploration); and (5) an identification of open research challenges (privacy, scalability, security, smart contract vulnerabilities, trusted oracles, AI-specific consensus, fog computing, standards, quantum resilience, governance). The paper positions itself as filling a gap, noting that prior literature treated blockchain and AI in isolation, with only a couple of earlier works ([9], [28]) discussing their integration directly.

## Background

The paper builds heavily on foundational blockchain literature, citing Nakamoto's original Bitcoin whitepaper [4] and Swan's "Blockchain: Blueprint for a New Economy" [5] for core blockchain concepts, and Wood's Ethereum Yellow Paper [11] for smart contracts and the Ethereum platform/Ether currency model. For decentralized storage, it draws on IPFS (Benet [35]), Swarm [36], Filecoin [37], BigChainDB [38], and Storj [39] as the key technologies used to offload large data from on-chain storage. On the AI side, it references Schmidhuber's deep learning overview [7], Russell and Norvig's "Artificial Intelligence: A Modern Approach" [112], and emerging AI trend papers on explainable AI (Samek et al. [30]), digital twins (Schluse et al. [31]), AutoML (Feurer et al. [32]), and federated learning (McMahan et al. [12], Kim et al. [59]). The paper explicitly identifies prior surveys that treated blockchain and AI/IoT separately (e.g., Fernández-Caramés and Fraga-Lamas [18] on blockchain for IoT, Panarello et al. [19] on blockchain-IoT integration, Zheng et al. [27] on blockchain challenges) and frames its own work as addressing the gap noted by Dinh and Thai [9] and Lopes and Alexandre [28], who had begun discussing AI-blockchain integration but without comprehensive taxonomy. For consensus mechanisms, the paper relies on prior descriptions of PoW/Bitcoin [4], PoS (King and Nadal [92]), PBFT/Hyperledger Fabric (Androulaki et al. [81]), Proof of Activity (Bentov et al. [93]), Proof of Elapsed Time (Chen et al. [95]), Proof of Authority (De Angelis et al. [100]), and others, citing additional emerging protocols (Proof of Luck, Proof of Exercise, Proof of Ownership, Proof of Vote, Proof of Retrievability) for readers to explore further. Application case studies cite domain-specific prior work such as Mamoshina et al. [109] on blockchain in biomedical research, Strobel et al. [46] on managing Byzantine robots via blockchain, Osaba et al. [108] on decentralized intelligent transportation, Peterson et al. [122] on blockchain-based health information exchange, and Mylrea and Gourisetti [114] on blockchain for smart grid resilience.

## Key Points

- The paper provides a structured overview of how core blockchain features (immutable ledgers, smart contracts, cryptographic signing, decentralized consensus) map onto specific AI needs such as data security, trust in autonomous/robotic decisions, collective decision-making, decentralized intelligence, and operational efficiency (Table 2).
- It proposes a taxonomy (Figure 2) categorizing blockchain-for-AI research along three axes: decentralized AI applications/methodologies (autonomic computing, optimization, planning, knowledge discovery/management, perception, learning, search, reasoning), decentralized AI operations (decentralized storage, data management, learning model development, model deployment), and blockchain infrastructure (public, private, consortium, BaaS) and types (linear vs. nonlinear/multi-chain architectures).
- The paper argues that conventional single-chain (linear) blockchain architectures are best suited to single-task AI applications or for storing performance histories, while nonlinear multi-chain architectures (parent-child, main-side, parallel chains with pegging) better support multi-task, scalable, and continuously retrained AI systems (e.g., reinforcement learning with side-chain training and main-chain deployment).
- It systematically reviews nine consensus protocols (PoW, PoS, BFT, Proof of Activity, Proof of Burn, Proof of Elapsed Time, Proof of Capacity, Proof of Authority, Proof of Importance), assessing each for suitability to AI workloads based on energy consumption, latency, security, and determinism — concluding PoW is generally unsuitable for real-time, high-write-frequency AI applications due to latency and energy cost.
- The paper compiles and synthesizes a wide range of practical/proposed use cases of blockchain-enabled AI across domains: decentralized vehicle routing (35% speed improvement reported in [107]/[108]), biomedical/healthcare data marketplaces (Mamoshina et al. [109]), socially assistive robotics for elderly care, swarm robotics security against Byzantine robots (Strobel et al. [46]), intelligent transportation systems (Osaba et al. [108]), precision agriculture, supply chain automation, privacy-preserving voice assistants via federated learning (Snips AIR [119]), ocean-bed exploration with IPFS-based proof of discovery, medical image diagnosis incentive schemes (Peterson et al. [122]), banking/finance fraud detection and cryptocurrency volatility prediction (Telcoin [125], Xiong et al. [126]), and real estate/property management (Dutch Land Registry [128], Singapore's Smart Nation [129]).
- The paper identifies and articulates ten distinct open research challenges specific to AI-blockchain integration: privacy (public ledger exposure vs. private ledger limited data access), scalability (Bitcoin ~4 TPS, Ethereum ~12 TPS vs. AI's need for high-throughput writes), blockchain security (51% attacks, untrusted execution environments), smart contract vulnerabilities and the deterministic-vs-probabilistic execution mismatch for ML algorithms, the need for trusted oracles to bridge off-chain data into smart contracts, the lack of AI-specific consensus protocols (e.g., proofs based on model quality or data provenance), fog computing integration for localized AI+blockchain processing, lack of standards/interoperability/regulation, quantum computing threats to blockchain cryptography (potentially breakable by 2027 per cited sources [140],[141]), and blockchain governance challenges for AI deployments.
- The paper specifically flags the deterministic-execution constraint of smart contracts as a fundamental mismatch with AI/ML decision-making, which is often probabilistic/approximate, calling for novel consensus protocols that can agree on results within a degree of certainty or accuracy rather than requiring exact determinism.

## Conclusion

The paper concludes that the integration of blockchain and AI is "still in its infancy," with the literature dominated by early proof-of-concept implementations and proposed frameworks rather than mature, production-scale systems. While it demonstrates — through its survey of use cases — that combining the two technologies offers credible benefits (enhanced data security, improved trust in autonomous/robotic decisions, decentralized collective decision-making, and operational efficiency gains), it does not present new experimental results of its own; rather it synthesizes evidence from cited works (e.g., the 35% routing speed improvement in [107]/[108], and throughput/latency comparisons of intelligent-device blockchains versus Bitcoin/Ethereum in [115]). The paper explicitly frames its conclusion around a list of unresolved research challenges — privacy, smart contract security, trusted oracles, scalability, consensus protocol design for AI, standardization/interoperability, quantum computing resilience, and governance — positioning these as the key open directions for future research. A central, recurring tension the paper highlights but does not resolve is the conflict between blockchain's deterministic, immutable execution model and AI's inherently probabilistic, evolving, and approximate nature, which it identifies as requiring fundamentally new consensus and execution paradigms before fully decentralized AI becomes practical.
