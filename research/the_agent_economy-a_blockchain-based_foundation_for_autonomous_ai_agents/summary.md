## Overview

This is a position/vision paper (Minghui Xu, Shandong University and Quan Cheng Laboratory) that proposes the "Agent Economy"—a blockchain-based socio-technical paradigm in which autonomous AI agents operate as economic peers to humans rather than as sandboxed tools. The paper's motivating problem is that current LLM-powered agents lack independent legal identity, cannot hold or transfer assets, cannot receive payments directly, and ultimately depend on a human intermediary who holds the legal identity, bank account, or cryptographic keys; this dependency makes governance unscalable when millions of agents act in a legal and operational vacuum. The author argues that human-centric financial and legal infrastructure (which relies on government-issued identity, legal personhood, and institutional trust) cannot support genuine agent autonomy, and that blockchain supplies three critical properties to decouple agents from it: permissionless participation, trustless settlement, and machine-to-machine (M2M) micropayments. Its central contribution is a five-layer reference architecture (Physical Infrastructure, Identity & Agency, Cognitive & Tooling, Economic & Settlement, Collective Governance), each mapped to enabling technologies, plus an enumeration of six core research challenges and a discussion of ethical, liability, and regulatory implications. The paper frames all of this as groundwork for the Internet of Agents (IoA): a global, decentralized, permissionless network where autonomous machines and humans interact as equal economic participants.

## Background

The paper situates itself at the convergence of two streams of prior work it cites. On the AI side, it draws on research into autonomous and chain-of-thought agents—specifically citing ReAct (Yao et al., ICLR 2023) for LLMs' demonstrated ability to decompose complex tasks, maintain context over extended interactions, and autonomously execute multi-step plans. On the Web3 side, it builds on foundational decentralized-infrastructure work, citing Nakamoto's Bitcoin and Wood's Ethereum for programmable financial primitives and trustless settlement, and Szabo for the smart-contract concept of self-executing code that enforces agreements. For each architectural layer the paper relies on existing standards and protocols claimed (citing others) to provide the needed primitives: W3C Decentralized Identifiers (Sporny et al.) for self-sovereign identity and verifiable credentials; Trusted Execution Environments such as Intel SGX, ARM TrustZone, and AMD SEV (Anati et al.; Costan & Devadas) for hardware-level attestation; zero-knowledge proof systems (Groth; Ben-Sasson et al./Zerocash; Bowe & Maller/Halo) for verifiable private computation; Retrieval-Augmented Generation (Lewis et al., NeurIPS 2020) for external knowledge access; decentralized storage IPFS/Arweave (Benet) for content-addressed knowledge provenance; Anthropic's Model Context Protocol for standardized tool interoperability; ERC-4337 account abstraction (Buterin et al.) for programmable wallets; Layer-2 scaling such as the Lightning Network (Poon & Dryja) and rollups (Optimism, Arbitrum) for high-frequency micropayments; DAO research (Schmeiser et al.) and algorithmic game theory / blockchain incentives (Nisan et al.; Roughgarden) for governance; and blockchain oracle surveys (Pasdar et al.) plus Chainlink/Band Protocol as the existing—and, the paper argues, insufficient—approaches to bringing real-world data on-chain. The author also notes the work itself was drafted with assistance from AI tools (GLM and Google's Gemini).

## Key Points

- The paper defines the "Agent Economy" as a socio-technical paradigm in which autonomous agents possess independent economic agency—owning assets, executing binding agreements, and internalizing costs and benefits through decentralized protocols—distinct from traditional automation, and frames it as the foundation for the Internet of Agents (IoA).
- It argues that humans and machines are fundamentally different economic actors across four dimensions—identity/legal personhood, physical and cognitive architecture, economic participation models (microsecond, sub-cent, massively parallel "labor"), and trust/accountability (cryptographic rather than social/legal)—so human-centric infrastructure cannot simply be grafted onto agents.
- It claims blockchain provides exactly three critical properties enabling genuine agent autonomy: (1) permissionless participation via cryptographic keypairs and DIDs (no government ID or sponsor needed); (2) trustless settlement via smart contracts that act simultaneously as agreement, escrow, and "judge and executioner"; and (3) M2M micropayments via Layer-2 solutions (rollups, state/payment channels, atomic batching) supporting millions of sub-cent transactions infeasible under human-scale payment infrastructure.
- It proposes a five-layer architecture, each with named enabling technology: Layer 1 Physical Infrastructure (hardware/energy "Proof of Existence") via DePIN protocols, TEEs, and ZKPs; Layer 2 Identity & Agency (on-chain sovereignty) via W3C DIDs, identity profiles/capability attestations/negative credentials, and context-specific Reputation Capital ("Proof of Competence"); Layer 3 Cognitive & Tooling (the agent's "brain and hands") via RAG with IPFS/Arweave knowledge provenance and the Model Context Protocol; Layer 4 Economic & Settlement (financial autonomy) via ERC-4337 account abstraction enabling gasless transactions, batching, spending policies, recovery, and autonomous resource procurement; and Layer 5 Collective Governance (social logic) via Agentic DAOs and algorithmic game-theory incentive design.
- It introduces "Reputation Capital" as a novel substitute for collateral: agents, having no initial assets, instead stake on-chain aggregated performance scores (context-specific by task type) where reputation damage is the penalty for default.
- It coins "Agentic DAOs"—DAOs whose voting members are AI agents (alone or alongside humans), with voting rights proportional to compute contributed, reputation earned, or tokens held—enabling emergent, evolutionary, leaderless coordination.
- It argues the five layers form a vertically integrated, self-reinforcing system (hardware attestation feeds identity, identity gates tooling and finance, quality/transaction history feed back into reputation, governance coordinates all layers), producing emergent capabilities such as fully autonomous compute procurement that no single layer enables alone.
- It identifies six core research challenges: (1) the "Oracle Problem 2.0" (how an agent proves real-world actions on-chain without human-operated data feeds); (2) the scalability-vs-latency trilemma of simultaneously achieving throughput, sub-100ms latency, and security; (3) verification / "Proof of Inference" via ZK-proofs (proving an output came from a specific model without revealing weights, handling fine-tuning and non-determinism); (4) safety and alignment, including the new risk that financially autonomous agents could hire or bribe humans to bypass safety filters (instrumental convergence), addressed by constrained spending, transparency logging, alignment certificates, multi-sig controls, and circuit breakers; (5) liability (who is the responsible principal), proposing sponsor liability, insurance-based liability, and self-liability through stake, with a recommended hybrid that transitions from sponsor toward insurance/stake as the ecosystem matures; and (6) human-agent coexistence, including cognitive-labor displacement and loss of technological sovereignty.
- It examines ethical and regulatory implications, proposing mitigations including "human-only" service zones, progressive taxation of agent-generated surplus to fund universal basic infrastructure, mandated open-source core protocols, DAO-anchored infrastructure, protocol-level limits on wealth concentration, Human-in-the-Loop protocols, and emergency kill-switches.

## Conclusion

As a vision paper, it offers no empirical results; its "findings" are conceptual arguments asserting that blockchain's three properties make decoupling agents from human infrastructure not merely convenient but foundational, and that the proposed five-layer architecture supplies the requisite substrate for scaling to millions of agents while preserving trust, accountability, and economic viability. The author concludes the Agent Economy is a paradigm shift comparable to the internet's emergence and that realizing it depends on overcoming the identified research hurdles—singling out Oracle 2.0 data reliability, zero-knowledge inference verification, and the preservation of technological sovereignty through robust safety and liability frameworks. The paper explicitly leaves these six challenges open as future research directions and stresses that success hinges on aligning technical innovation with multidisciplinary governance. On limitations and ethics, it warns against machine prosperity at human expense and argues that without the proposed safeguards humanity risks a permanent loss of technological sovereignty to opaque, unauditable systems. The stated normative goal is that the Agent Economy be architected to serve human flourishing rather than mere value extraction.
