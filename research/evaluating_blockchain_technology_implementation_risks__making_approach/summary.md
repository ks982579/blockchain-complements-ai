## Overview

This paper (Hosseini Dehshiri & Amiri, 2026, *Industrial Management Journal*, 18(2), 237-264) identifies and prioritizes the risks of adopting blockchain technology in the supply chains of Small and Medium-sized Enterprises (SMEs) in the automotive (auto-parts manufacturing) industry. The authors argue that while blockchain offers transparency, traceability, immutability, and security benefits for supply chains, its adoption carries substantial implementation risks that must be systematically assessed under uncertainty. Through a literature review and expert consultation, they compile a hierarchy of 18 sub-risks grouped into five main categories (organizational, security, technical, financial/commercial, environmental/cultural), then weight them using a fuzzy Multi-Criteria Decision-Making (MCDM) approach combining triangular fuzzy numbers with the Stepwise Weight Assessment Ratio Analysis (SWARA) method. Applying the framework to an Iranian automotive-SME case study (a 5-expert decision team with 10+ years of experience), they find security, technical, and organizational risks are the most important main categories, and privacy/confidentiality of information (0.1224), lack of necessary policies and regulations (0.0933), and cyber-attacks (0.0927) are the most critical sub-risks. The contribution is a comprehensive, group-based, uncertainty-aware risk prioritization framework that fills a gap left by prior largely review-based blockchain risk studies.

## Background

The paper builds on a body of prior work it cites to motivate and frame its own argument. It notes that blockchain was introduced by Nakamoto (2008) as a component of Bitcoin (a decentralized peer-to-peer electronic payment system) and has since expanded to broader economic, commercial, and supply-chain applications (citing Hughes et al., 2019; Hawlitschek et al., 2018; Kouhizadeh et al., 2021). The authors cite Saberi et al. (2019) for the claim that blockchain adoption barriers in supply chains fall into four categories—inter-organizational, intra-organizational, technical, and external. They draw on prior risk/barrier studies including Zheng et al. (2018) on technical risks and consensus algorithms, Kouhizadeh et al. (2021) who used DEMATEL to identify technical barriers as the main hindrance, Özkan et al. (2020) who used Pythagorean fuzzy hierarchy analysis and found security risks most important, Khan et al. (2023) who used BWM for food-supply-chain barriers, Çıkmak et al. (2024) who used Interval Type-2 Fuzzy AHP/TOPSIS (highlighting decentralization, security, immutability), Salaj & Kumari (2025) on Entropy-CRITIC MCDM for blockchain selection, and Abdollahi et al. (2026) on fuzzy ZE-number frameworks for solar-energy supply chains. The fuzzy-set theory it uses traces to Zadeh (1965) and Zimmermann (2011), the SWARA method to Keršuliene et al. (2010), and fuzzy SWARA application to Mavi et al. (2017). The specific 18 risks and their descriptions are sourced from references such as Kim & Kang (2017), Böhme et al. (2015), Almutairi et al. (2022), Yadav et al. (2020), Litke et al. (2019), and Janssen et al. (2020). The paper identifies a research gap: most prior studies were review-based, few prioritized risks in a case study, many ignored uncertainty in expert evaluation, and few used a group MCDM approach incorporating all stakeholders.

## Key Points

- The paper identifies and finalizes 18 blockchain implementation sub-risks for automotive-industry SMEs, organized into five main risk categories: organizational (R1), security (R2), technical (R3), financial/commercial (R4), and environmental/cultural (R5).
- Among the five main risk categories, security (R2, crisp weight 0.339), technical (R3, 0.254), and organizational (R1, 0.203) were ranked as the most important, followed by environmental/cultural (R5, 0.136) and financial/commercial (R4, 0.068).
- Across all 18 sub-risks, the three most critical were privacy/confidentiality of information (R22, 0.1224), lack of necessary policies and regulations (R14, 0.0933), and cyber-attacks (R21, 0.0927).
- Within the security category, the ranked sub-risks were privacy/confidentiality (R22, 0.122), cyber-attacks (R21, 0.093), private key security/key management (R24, 0.072), and illegal activity/criminal abuse (R23, 0.057).
- Within the organizational category, lack of necessary policies and regulations (R14, 0.093) ranked highest, followed by lack of necessary infrastructure (R13, 0.071), lack of skilled employees (R12, 0.040), and lack of management support (R11, 0.033).
- Within the technical category, poor scalability (R34, 0.089) ranked highest, followed by irreversibility (R31, 0.069), consensus protocol inefficiency (R33, 0.055), and lack of integration with other systems (R32, 0.046).
- Within the financial/commercial category, high usage expense (R41, 0.039) outranked training cost (R42, 0.031); within environmental/cultural, non-acceptance of legal authorities and lack of regulatory laws (R53, 0.046) ranked highest, followed by customer uncertainty (R51, 0.038), problems in cooperation/communication/coordination (R54, 0.030), and lack of industry involvement (R52, 0.024).
- The paper demonstrates that integrating triangular fuzzy numbers with the SWARA method (FSWARA) provides a systematic, group-based, uncertainty-aware approach for assessing blockchain adoption risks, claiming improved robustness and accuracy over non-fuzzy or non-group methods because SWARA confirms experts' weighting opinions through collaboration.
- The paper claims its results are case-dependent: for the studied firm, risks like inter-member cooperation, consensus mechanism inefficiency, and training cost were lower priority due to firm-specific conditions (effective local supply-chain relationships, suitable proof-of-work consensus, management support, and skilled employees).

## Conclusion

The study concludes that the FSWARA framework successfully identified and prioritized 18 blockchain implementation risks under uncertainty, with security, technical, and organizational risks emerging as the dominant categories, and privacy/confidentiality (0.1224), lack of necessary policies and regulations (0.0933), and cyber-attacks (0.0927) as the top sub-risks. The authors position these findings as practical guidance enabling supply-chain managers and policymakers to focus limited financial and time resources on the highest-priority risks and to pursue sustainable competitive advantage. The paper notes its results are partly consistent with prior work (e.g., scalability and privacy as top risks per Sanka et al., 2021; Drljevic et al., 2020; Zheng et al., 2018; Litke et al., 2019), while diverging where firm-specific conditions reduced certain risks' importance. The authors acknowledge blockchain risk-management research is still in its infancy and recommend several future-research directions: examining blockchain risks in other sectors (energy, food) and in developing countries; studying new risks that emerge as blockchain evolves; addressing policy/legal implications, infrastructure challenges, high data-storage costs with low-latency processing, scalability, and consensus-mechanism optimization; using exploratory/confirmatory factor analysis or fuzzy Delphi to validate the risk hierarchy; applying DEMATEL or ANP to capture cause-effect relationships among risks; and developing new MCDM methods incorporating mixed uncertainty types such as rough numbers, distance fuzzy numbers, and Z-numbers. A key limitation is that the prioritization is grounded in a single-firm, expert-judgment-based case study, making some weightings context-specific.
