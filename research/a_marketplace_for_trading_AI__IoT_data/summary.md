## Overview

This paper proposes a Blockchain-based marketplace for trading and collaboratively improving AI/ML models using IoT-generated data, targeting the federated learning (FL) setting where "buyers" publish models needing training and "sellers" (IoT devices) contribute local computation on their private data in exchange for incentives. The work addresses two intertwined gaps in FL-based data/model markets: the lack of a trustworthy, decentralized infrastructure to coordinate model exchange and payment (avoiding the single point of failure and manipulation risk of a centralized aggregator/platform), and the lack of a fair, efficient mechanism to value each participant's data contribution for incentive payouts. The system is built on a permissionless Ethereum blockchain with smart contracts governing deposits, model transmission, training, aggregation, settlement, and token-based payment channels, with raw models/data stored off-chain via IPFS and only hashes recorded on-chain for scalability. Its core technical contribution is an Approximate Federated Shapley Value (AFS) algorithm—an engineered variant of Truncated Monte-Carlo Shapley value estimation tailored to FL—that reconstructs models from per-round gradient updates to estimate each seller's marginal contribution without the prohibitive cost of retraining on all 2^N data-source combinations, and the paper derives a Hoeffding-based performance bound relating sampling size to approximation error. Experiments on a prototype testbed (Ethereum/Ganache, IPFS, five Raspberry Pi devices, MNIST dataset across four data-distribution scenarios) show AFS achieves competitive or superior accuracy-vs-runtime trade-offs against exact Shapley, Single-Cal, and Multi-Cal baselines, the overall approach achieves competitive run-time performance with roughly a 15% drop in the cost of execution, and incentive allocations track each seller's data quantity (dataset size) fairly across scenarios, with data quality-based valuation explicitly left out of scope. Overall, the paper's contribution is an integrated, empirically validated ecosystem combining DLT-based trust/transparency with a computationally tractable, FL-adapted Shapley valuation scheme to enable fair, transparent ML model trading among heterogeneous IoT participants.

## Background

This paper situates its proposed Blockchain-based ML model marketplace within several strands of prior work, which it cites to motivate the design and to position its contributions.

**Growth of IoT data and the data economy.** The paper opens by citing an IDC industry forecast (Reisinger/IDC report [1]) projecting that IoT devices will generate 79.4 Zettabytes of data globally by 2025, framing the scale of the underlying problem. It also points to prior work on exploiting IoT data for business workflows and data-sharing platforms (Huang et al. [2]; Perera's "Sensing as a Service" concept [3]; Mao, Zheng, and Wu on IoT data market pricing via information design [4]), arguing that emerging platforms for accessing/sharing IoT data are already connecting distributed sources and enabling suppliers to exchange data.

**Air quality monitoring as a motivating use case.** To ground the motivation, the paper draws on the Air Quality Index (AQI) literature, citing comparative studies of AQI estimation methods (Bishoi et al. [5]), sensing-based IoT air quality platforms (Jo et al. [6]), vision-based/UAV federated-learning air quality sensing (Liu et al. [7]), and dense sensor network feasibility studies (Moltchanov et al. [8]). These are used to illustrate the tension between data quality/quantity requirements, energy costs, single-point-of-failure server architectures, and privacy regulation.

**Data privacy regulation.** The paper cites work surveying privacy regulations such as GDPR, CCPA, and China's Cyber Security Law (Gruschka et al. [9]) as the regulatory backdrop that constrains naive centralized data collection and motivates privacy-preserving training approaches like federated learning.

**Federated Learning (FL) foundations.** The core FL paradigm and the FedAvg algorithm the paper builds on are attributed to McMahan et al.'s seminal "Communication-efficient learning of deep networks from decentralized data" [10]. The paper also cites the broad FL survey by Kairouz et al. [21] for general FL background and open problems, and Xu et al. [11] for the argument that a shared aggregator/IT environment can become a single point of failure for data integrity, trust, security, and transparency — a key justification for introducing DLT.

**Centralized data markets and their shortcomings.** The paper cites prior centralized data-market/service-platform designs (Radhakrishnan and Krishnamachari's streaming data payment protocol [12]; Niu et al. on data truthfulness and privacy in data markets [13]) as examples of the conventional approach it seeks to improve upon, highlighting concerns about single points of security failure and lack of incentive mechanisms for honest participation.

**Incentive mechanisms for FL/data sharing.** The paper draws on work concerning incentives and fraud in markets generally (Langevoort on fraud/insider trading in securities regulation [14]) and specifically on crowdsourcing/incentive frameworks for on-device federated learning (Pandey et al. [15]), supporting the claim that standard FL lacks natural incentive structures and that careful mechanism design is needed to reward quality contributions and discourage low-quality or reused data.

**Blockchain/DLT foundations.** The foundational reference for Blockchain/DLT itself is Nakamoto's Bitcoin whitepaper [16]. The paper also relies on its own prior work on Blockchain-based IoT data markets (Nguyen et al., "Modeling and analysis of data trading on blockchain-based market in IoT networks" [17]) and on trusted wireless monitoring using distributed ledgers over NB-IoT (Nguyen et al. [19]), as well as Chen et al. on blockchain-based proxy re-encryption for vehicular systems [18] and Wang et al.'s analysis of the Ethereum peer-to-peer network [20], to support claims about DLTs enabling immutable, transparent, trustless recordkeeping, eliminating third parties, and preventing tampering/injection of fake data.

**Combining FL with DLT.** The integration of FL with blockchain to remove single-server dependency is attributed to Kim, Park, Bennis, and Kim's work on "Blockchained on-device federated learning" [22], and Ding et al. [26] is cited regarding incentive mechanisms for secure blockchain-based IoT and Proof-of-Work block generation/fork resolution (referencing Bitcoin/Ethereum PoW conventions).

**Shapley Value and data valuation.** The game-theoretic foundation for fair contribution-based payoff allocation is the classical Shapley Value, attributed to Roth's edited volume on Shapley value theory [23]. For computing Shapley values efficiently in ML/FL settings, the paper relies heavily on Jia et al.'s "Towards efficient data valuation based on the Shapley value" [25] (source of the Truncated Monte Carlo, TMC, approximation method and the performance-bound proof technique via Hoeffding's theorem [30]), and Ghorbani and Zou's "Data Shapley: Equitable valuation of data for machine learning" [28]. Qu et al. [27] is cited regarding the communication/computation costs of Shapley-based valuation in decentralized federated learning. Charles and Konečný [29] is cited on the importance of decaying learning rates, used to justify why the order of client contributions matters in FL Shapley computation.

**Ethereum and smart contract platform.** The experimental implementation builds on the Ethereum platform and Ethereum Yellow Paper (Wood et al. [24]) for its account of the Ethereum Virtual Machine, gas costs, and smart contract execution, plus the Ganache personal blockchain tool and Remix IDE for development/testing.

**Related work on blockchain-based asset/data trading (Section V).** The paper surveys several prior marketplace designs it positions itself against: Gupta, Kanhere, and Jurdak's decentralized IoT data marketplace with a three-tier (provider/broker/consumer) architecture and reputation system [33]; work on revenue-maximization with envy-free guarantees showing APX-hardness and logarithmic approximation results (cited as [34], though the reference list entry under that number appears to actually correspond to a differential privacy paper by Iyengar et al. — a possible citation mismatch worth checking); Bajoudah, Dong, and Missier's decentralized, trustless IoT data trading marketplace with periodic checkpoints against fraud [35]; Missier et al.'s "Mind My Value" infrastructure using blockchain oracles for off-chain queries [36]; and Xiong and Xiong's smart-contract-based data trading model incorporating arbitration and AI/ML for fairness [31].

**Related work on data valuation (Section V).** The paper cites Wang, Rausch, Zhang, Jia, and Song's "principled approach to data valuation for federated learning" [37] for a taxonomy of data pricing strategies: query-based pricing (citing Kairouz et al. [21] and Leroy et al. on federated keyword spotting [38], and Upadhyaya et al. on price-optimal querying with data APIs [39]); attribute-based pricing via public price registries (Heckman et al. [40]); and auction-based pricing (Mihailescu and Teo on dynamic resource pricing for federated clouds [41]; Lee and Hoh on market-based incentives for participatory sensing [42]). It further cites applications of Shapley Value to feature importance and explainability in ML (Cohen, Dror, and Ruppin [43]; Štrumbelj and Kononenko [44]), the concept of distributional Shapley Value drawing on Aumann and Shapley's "Values of Non-Atomic Games" [45], and Tang et al.'s application of Shapley Value to medical imaging data valuation, which found that removing low-Shapley-value data improves model performance while removing high-value data degrades it [46]. Niu et al. [13] and Agarwal, Dahleh, and Sarkar's algorithmic marketplace for data [32] are also cited (alongside [31]) regarding mechanisms for handling dishonest/untruthful data reporting in FL settings, which the paper notes is an open issue beyond its own scope.

Overall, the paper situates its contribution at the intersection of three established research threads — federated learning (McMahan et al. [10]), blockchain/DLT-based trust infrastructures (Nakamoto [16], Wood et al. [24], and the authors' own prior IoT-blockchain work [17], [19]) — and Shapley-value-based data valuation (Jia et al. [25], Ghorbani and Zou [28]), combining and extending these to address gaps in fairness, trust, and incentive design that it argues are insufficiently handled by existing centralized data markets and standard FL/Shapley approaches.

## Key Points

- This paper proposes a Blockchain-based ML model marketplace in which buyers acquire models for training and sellers (IoT devices) contribute local computation on private data, with all trading details recorded in a tamper-proof distributed ledger.
- The system is built on a permissionless Ethereum blockchain with smart contracts handling deposits, payments, and settlement, while raw data and trained model files are stored off-chain via IPFS and only hashes/digests are recorded on-chain to reduce storage and execution costs.
- The paper introduces AFS (Approximate Federated Shapley value), a new algorithm based on the "Single-Cal" approach but using Truncated Monte-Carlo (TMC) sampling, which estimates each participant's data Shapley Value by reusing gradient information from federated training rounds, avoiding the need to retrain models on every subset of clients.
- This paper shows that directly computing the standard/exact Federated Shapley Value (SFSV) is computationally prohibitive for federated learning because it requires training and evaluating models on combinations (2^n) of client datasets.
- The paper formally derives a performance bound (via Hoeffding's theorem) on the AFS estimator, showing that for a known range v of marginal contributions, a minimum permutation sample size |S| guarantees an error ε with confidence (1-α).
- This paper demonstrates experimentally (on MNIST with Raspberry Pi devices and an Ethereum/Ganache testbed) that AFS achieves competitive run-time performance compared to exact Shapley Value computation, Single-Cal, and Multi-Cal methods, while maintaining low maximum-difference (D_max) error relative to the exact SV.
- The paper reports that, in scenario 4 with noisier data, the Multi-Cal baseline shows better results than the other methods, achieving approximately a 10% improvement in run-time and approximately a 15% lower maximum-difference (D_max) value.
- This paper provides an empirical comparison of standard FL versus DLT-based FL accuracy and convergence on FL-MNIST and FL-CIFAR10, showing similar final accuracy but slower convergence for DLT-based FL due to added verification and consensus overhead.
- The paper reports measured Ethereum smart contract execution (gas) costs in Gwei/ETH/USD for core marketplace operations (AddWorker, ModelTransmission, ModelTraining, ModelAggregation, Settlement, PayChannelExecute) on a Ganache-based testbed.
- This paper demonstrates, via experiments across four dataset distribution scenarios (equal size, unequal size, non-IID class distribution, and noisy data), that incentive payments to sellers (in tokens) scale proportionally with each participant's dataset size and contribution, providing empirical evidence of fairness in the incentive mechanism.
- The paper argues that combining DLT with Federated Learning addresses the single-point-of-failure and trust limitations of conventional centralized data/model marketplaces and FL aggregators, while preserving data privacy by only exchanging models (not raw data) between participants.
- This paper's ten-step communication workflow (model initialization, publishing, download, local training, ledger update, cross-verification/consensus, block propagation, settlement, incentive distribution, and receipt recording) defines a concrete protocol for trustworthy, blockchain-mediated federated model training and trading.

## Conclusion

The paper set out to design and validate a Blockchain-based marketplace for trading AI/ML models among heterogeneous IoT participants, with two central claims: (1) that a DLT-based system can provide trusted, transparent, and tamper-proof transactions for federated model trading without a centralized aggregator as a single point of failure, and (2) that a distributed Shapley Value method can fairly and efficiently value sellers' data contributions to incentivize participation. The authors conclude that both claims are supported by their proof-of-concept implementation. They built the marketplace on a permissionless Ethereum network (via Ganache), with smart contracts governing trades, payments, and settlement, off-chain storage via IPFS for raw models/data, and Raspberry Pi devices acting as sellers training on MNIST data.

On the incentive/valuation side, the proposed Approximate Federated Shapley value (AFS) algorithm — an engineered derivative of Truncated Monte-Carlo sampling applied to standard Federated Shapley Value — was shown to avoid the costly retraining required by exact Shapley Value computation while still producing contribution estimates close to the exact values, particularly in scenarios with non-uniform data sizes and noisy data (Scenarios 2 and 4), where competing methods (Single-Cal, Multi-Cal, Exact) performed worse or more erratically. The incentive experiments (Fig. 7) confirmed that token payouts to sellers tracked their relative dataset sizes/contributions as expected, supporting the "fair payment" design goal. On the blockchain side, the smart-contract execution cost results and overall runtime comparisons showed the system is competitive, with the paper highlighting an approximate 15% reduction in execution cost relative to baseline/exact approaches, alongside maintained model accuracy comparable to standard FL (with some increase in convergence time due to consensus/verification overhead, as shown in Fig. 3).

The paper is candid about several limitations and leaves a number of open research directions:

- **Data quality vs. quantity**: The DSV/AFS approach as implemented quantifies a seller's contribution primarily via dataset *size* rather than intrinsic data *quality* or feature richness. The authors explicitly note that for applications like healthcare, valuation based on actual data quality (not just quantity) would be preferable, but this is left out of scope.
- **Truthfulness and dishonest reporting**: The system assumes sellers report honestly, but the paper acknowledges that sellers could submit poor-quality or fabricated data to game the incentive mechanism. While the DLT can track contributions and gradient/SV relationships to help detect such behavior, a detailed mechanism for handling untruthful reporting is identified as future work (pointing to prior literature on truthful data markets as a starting point).
- **Computational trade-offs among SV approximation methods**: The Multi-Cal method, while more accurate in some noisy scenarios, is more computationally expensive than Single-Cal/AFS, suggesting an open design space for adaptively selecting or further optimizing valuation algorithms depending on data distribution characteristics.
- **Scalability and consensus overhead**: The DLT-based FL setup incurs additional convergence time relative to standard FL due to verification and consensus (PoW-based mining, fork resolution), an overhead that is not deeply analyzed for larger networks or alternative consensus mechanisms.
- **Generality of the marketplace design**: Experiments were limited to a small-scale testbed (five IoT devices, MNIST dataset) and a single-buyer/multi-seller framing; the paper does not explore multi-buyer competition, more complex/large-scale models (beyond MNIST/CIFAR-10 mentioned for baseline FL comparisons), or richer pricing/negotiation mechanisms beyond the basic deposit-and-settlement smart contract flow.

Overall, the conclusion frames the work as a feasibility demonstration — an end-to-end ecosystem combining DLT-based trust, smart-contract-driven incentives, and an efficient Shapley-based valuation scheme — rather than a fully production-ready or exhaustively benchmarked system, with truthfulness mechanisms, quality-aware valuation, and larger-scale evaluation flagged as natural next steps.
