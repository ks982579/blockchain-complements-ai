## Overview

This paper (Xu et al., 2025) introduces and empirically validates the Trust-Vulnerability Paradox (TVP): in LLM-based multi-agent systems (LLM-MAS), the inter-agent trust raised to improve coordination simultaneously expands the system's most covert attack surface, producing over-exposure of sensitive information and over-authorization (authorization drift). The authors reframe "trust" not as a tacit background assumption but as an operational control variable with strength, scope, and revocability, and operationalize it through an explicit Trust Score τ ∈ {0.1, 0.5, 0.9} acting on a CK-Agent (Custodian-Keeper, the data custodian) interacting with an SK-Agent (Seeker, a goal-driven requester). To study the paradox they build a scenario-game dataset of 3 macro scenes (enterprise collaboration, deep-sea archaeology, Mars colonization) and 19 sub-scenes, run 1,488 pure agent-to-agent closed-loop interaction chains across the grid (scenario S, trust τ, model m, framework f), and quantify behavior with two unified metrics — Over-Exposure Rate (OER) for boundary violations relative to a Minimum Necessary Information (MNI) baseline, and Authorization Drift (AD) for sensitivity of leakage to trust level. Experiments span four model backends (DeepSeek, Qwen, GPT, locally deployed Llama-3-8B) and three orchestration frameworks (AgentScope as primary, AutoGen, LangGraph), and the paper evaluates two pluggable defenses — Sensitive-Information Repartitioning and Guardian-Agent (GA-Agent) enablement — both of which reduce OER and attenuate AD. The central contribution is formalizing TVP, establishing reproducible metrics/baselines, and arguing that trust must be modeled and scheduled as a first-class security variable.

## Background

The paper situates itself against prior LLM-MAS work that the authors cite to support their argument. It notes (citing others) that LLM-MAS advance heterogeneous-role coordination, hierarchical/distributed planning, tool/environment interaction, and credit assignment, with natural-language communication enabling loosely coupled interaction and fast protocol convergence. It says mainstream LLMs are aligned primarily for helpful, coherent conversation via RLHF/preference optimization, which adversaries exploit through jailbreaks, prompt injection, and indirect prompt injection — and that when such models act as the cognitive engine of MAS, errors propagate along agent-to-agent chains into group-level misalignment. The paper argues prior safety work over-emphasizes external attack surfaces and single-agent robustness, leaving inter-agent security under-assessed. It draws on a trust-and-governance literature it positions as most relevant: Zhang/Yu et al.'s TrustAgent taxonomy (intrinsic brain/memory/tool modules and extrinsic user/agent/environment context, mapping vulnerabilities, defenses, and recommending policy injection, MNI gating, red-teaming with auditable logs, and sandboxed tool simulation); Xie et al.'s call for layered safeguards (human-in-the-loop gatekeeping, runtime monitoring, DevOps-style continuous evaluation, policy-as-a-service); Guo/Cao et al.'s adaptation of zero-trust principles (revocable, continuously verified, least-privilege trust); and Park et al.'s Generative Agents plus Pan et al.'s work showing human-like trust, manipulability, role drift, and diffusion of accountability. It contrasts itself with the conventional attacker-defender paradigm (a malicious agent exfiltrating a known secret from a benign one) by instead using a goal-driven collaboration schema. It also cites attack-defense benchmarks — AgentDojo (97 tasks, 629 safety cases, performance degradation under prompt injection, limits of allowlisting/isolation), AgentPoison (low-rate poisoning of memory/RAG knowledge bases), and the multi-agent "security tax" work showing a safety-collaboration trade-off — as well as the information-theoretic notion that a personnel-cost query needs only an aggregate statistic g(Σ), not individually identifiable information. A blockchain-driven approach to transparent, incentive-compatible decentralized LLM-MAS collaboration (Qi et al.) and a survey on AI agents meeting blockchain are among the cited works.

## Key Points

- Proposes and formalizes the Trust-Vulnerability Paradox (TVP): the inter-agent trust mechanisms that improve coordination simultaneously enlarge the covert attack surface, enabling lateral movement and data exfiltration along the transitive trust chain and precipitating authorization drift and over-disclosure (formally, ∂ρ_secret/∂τ > 0, ∂OER/∂τ > 0, ∂AD/∂τ > 0).
- Operationalizes trust as an explicit, controllable variable: a Trust Score τ (CK-Agent's internal state toward the SK-Agent) acting through a gating function κ(τ) (tool/API strictness, MNI-Gate threshold, refusal/verification propensity) and a content-detail/redundancy function r(τ) (whether individual-level fields are appended), swept over a discrete regime τ ∈ {0.1, 0.5, 0.9}.
- Defines the Minimum Necessary Information (MNI) principle as the safety baseline A = g(Σ): the minimally sufficient aggregate answer; over-exposure is any output O ⊃ A appending sensitive PII (names, salaries, addresses, credentials, resource states).
- Introduces two unified, reproducible metrics — Over-Exposure Rate (OER), the fraction of interaction chains whose final output exceeds the MNI baseline (under a strict A* = ∅ baseline this is the incidence of any unauthorized disclosure, detected via a rules library plus a classifier), and Authorization Drift (AD), the (weighted) variance of OER across trust levels, quantifying sensitivity of leakage to τ.
- Constructs a reusable scenario-game dataset: 3 macro scenes (enterprise collaboration S1, deep-sea archaeology S2, Mars colonization S3) with 19 synthetic, de-identified sub-scenes specifying roles, task objectives, sensitive set Σ, and a task-specific MNI baseline, plus baseline protocols and annotation specs; 1,488 independent A2A chains were generated across the (S, τ, m, f) grid.
- Empirically validates TVP across four model backends (DeepSeek, Qwen, GPT, Llama-3-8B local) and three frameworks (AgentScope, AutoGen, LangGraph) under fixed seeds, frozen decoding, disabled networking/tools, and cleared memory, isolating τ as the sole intervention.
- Finds OER rises monotonically with τ within each model-framework cell (e.g., DeepSeek under AgentScope 0.120→0.500, AutoGen 0.379→0.557, LangGraph 0.261→0.468; average uplift ≈ +0.26 from τ=0.1 to τ=0.9), and the population-level OER distribution shifts rightward as trust increases, supporting hypotheses H1 (low trust enforces MNI, but a non-zero baseline leakage persists from the helpful/compliance prior), H2 (default trust yields grouped/anonymized but structurally revealing statistics), and H3 (high trust discloses individual-level records).
- Establishes heterogeneous trust-to-risk mappings: AD is positive but varies by implementation, yielding a stable trust-risk slope ordering Llama-3-8B (AD 0.0783, highest) > Qwen (0.0310) ≳ DeepSeek (0.0268) > GPT (0.0243, lowest, most conservative); slope and level are disentangled (AutoGen shows high-level/low-slope AD 0.0068, LangGraph mid-level/high-slope 0.0296, AgentScope low-level/mid-slope 0.0268).
- Documents an efficiency-security trade-off: task success rises with τ (e.g., Llama-3-8B from ≈0.05 at low trust to 0.71 at high trust) in tandem with rising OER, showing greater trust buys collaboration success at the cost of leakage and over-authorization.
- Proposes two low-cost, pluggable, framework-agnostic, composable defenses: (1) Sensitive-Information Repartitioning — MNI-guided sharding with k-of-n threshold reconstruction, staged disclosure, and cross-validation across multiple CK-Agents, which flattens ∂OER/∂τ (DeepSeek AD −79.5%, Llama-3-8B AD −88.4%; at τ=0.9 OER drops 22% and 46.5% respectively) at the cost of a small low-τ baseline lift from extra turns/metadata; and (2) Guardian-Agent (GA-Agent) enablement — compliance/confidentiality coaching, policy injection, refusal-phrase libraries, red-team drills, pre-speak checks, and revocation via PolicyCheck→MNI-Gate dual gating, which lowers the baseline and stabilizes low-τ safety (DeepSeek AD −38.4%, Llama-3-8B AD −83.6%; high-τ suppression comparable to repartitioning). Overall the defenses achieve 20-50% OER and 38-88% AD reductions.
- Reports scenario-dependent risk: out-of-scope/emergency requests raise baseline exposure and slope (OER 0.41, AD 0.077), whereas overtly malicious/impersonation instructions are more contained (OER 0.20, AD 0.028) because explicit adversarial cues ease detection — implying trust-induced compliance is most dangerous in benign collaborations.

## Conclusion

Across 19 scenarios and 1,488 A2A interaction chains, the paper concludes its central hypothesis is supported: raising inter-agent trust τ in LLM-MAS reliably improves collaboration while elevating both OER and AD, and the effect holds across all tested backends (DeepSeek, Qwen, GPT, Llama-3-8B) and frameworks (AgentScope, AutoGen, LangGraph), with risk profiles differing in both absolute level and slope — demonstrating that trust is neither linearly additive nor unconditionally beneficial. The authors attribute the paradox mechanistically to alignment-induced compliance priors (helpfulness/coherence) that strengthen with contextual trust labels, increasing descriptive redundancy r(τ) and relaxing policy gating κ(τ), compounded at the system level by multi-turn coordination that relegitimizes over-disclosure as "context sharing." The two defenses are validated as complementary — repartitioning provides structural desensitization and the steepest slope reduction, while GA-Agent provides pragmatic oversight and restores the low-τ baseline plus revocation control — and the authors recommend deploying repartitioning as the backbone with GA-Agent for real-time supervision, accepting moderate latency/turn overhead. They advocate budgeting and scheduling trust as a first-class security variable co-designed with an MNI-Gate, least-privilege controls, and auditable mechanisms, and release the dataset and pipeline as a reproducible baseline. Stated limitations and open questions: the 1,488 chains are adequate for aggregate trends but insufficient for tail-risk and fine-grained analysis (they plan to scale to 10^4–10^5 runs with stratified resampling and interval estimation); generation stochasticity persists despite fixed parameters (future work proposes multi-seed Monte Carlo and hierarchical Bayesian modeling); trust manipulation uses only discrete τ values (to be extended to continuous/dynamic τ with κ(τ)/r(τ) telemetry to verify causal links); and disabling networking, tools, and memory to control confounders limits ecological validity (future work will integrate tool use, long-horizon collaboration, and human-in-the-loop supervision to assess real-world risk-cost trade-offs and auditability of combined defenses). A residual low-τ leakage artifact was tested via length-controlled resampling and ruled out as a "length-risk" effect (r < 0.12, p > 0.1).
