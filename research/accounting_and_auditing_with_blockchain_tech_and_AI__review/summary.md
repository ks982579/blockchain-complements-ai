## Overview

This is a systematic literature review (179 journal articles, 2017-2021) examining how
blockchain technology, combined with AI, is changing accounting and auditing practice.
The authors identify four emerging themes -- event-approach accounting, real-time
accounting, triple-entry accounting, and continuous auditing -- and interpret these
through agency theory and stakeholder theory. The contribution is a thematic synthesis
of how blockchain's properties (immutability, append-only, multi-party validation,
consensus) reduce information asymmetry and enable AI-driven audit automation, plus a
catalog of adoption challenges and future research questions for the accounting field.

## Background

The review draws on prior framings of blockchain as a "fifth pillar of the IT
revolution" (Thakkar, 2019) and an "Internet of value" (Tapscott & Euchner, 2019), and
cites industry estimates (World Economic Forum, PwC, Deloitte) projecting large-scale
GDP impact from blockchain by 2027-2030. It builds on agency theory (Jensen & Meckling,
1976) and stakeholder theory (Freeman, 1984/1994) as the interpretive lenses, and
references prior accounting-technology work (e.g., Dai & Vasarhelyi's 2017
blockchain-based accounting/assurance proposals, Sorter's 1969 event-approach
accounting theory, Cong et al. 2018 on technological disruption in accounting) as the
foundations for the four themes identified.

## Key Points

- Blockchain's qualities (immutability, append-only, shared, verified, consensus-driven
  data) provide multi-party-validated, real-time data that AI-based audit systems can
  trust, improving assurance and efficiency.
- Four themes describe blockchain's impact on accounting record-keeping: (1) the event
  approach to accounting (raw, disaggregated transaction data vs. aggregated reporting),
  (2) real-time accounting (instant, multi-party-validated balance sheets/ledgers), (3)
  triple-entry accounting (a shared, cryptographically-signed third ledger entry that
  improves reconciliation and trust), and (4) continuous auditing (AI + blockchain
  enabling always-on, automated audit procedures).
- Through an agency-theory lens, blockchain plus AI makes it harder for managers to
  manipulate accounting data and easier to detect anomalies/fraud in real time --
  though this assumes the underlying raw data entering the chain is itself honest (a
  51%-attack or "garbage in" risk remains).
- Through a stakeholder-theory lens, blockchain's distributed, shared-ledger nature can
  promote a more open, inclusive, collaborative accounting ecosystem among managers,
  accountants, auditors, investors, and regulators.
- Blockchain adoption faces technical, organizational, and legal challenges: energy
  consumption, storage, privacy, scalability, interoperability, cybersecurity, lack of
  standardization (e.g., unclear interplay with XBRL reporting standards), and
  significant change-management costs.
- The choice between permissionless, permissioned, and consortium blockchains involves
  a security/throughput trade-off; permissioned/consortium models are more likely to be
  adopted first in mainstream business for confidentiality, access control, and lower
  consensus costs.
- The roles of accountants/auditors will shift toward higher-value work (smart contract
  review, predictive audits, real-time fraud detection, software/algorithm audits)
  rather than being replaced outright; smaller accounting practices (SMPs) risk falling
  behind larger firms (Big 4) in blockchain/AI adoption, widening a competence gap.

## Conclusion

The review concludes that blockchain combined with AI can meaningfully improve
transparency, trust, and efficiency in accounting and auditing record-keeping, and that
agency and stakeholder theories both support this argument -- blockchain reduces
information asymmetry and enables broader stakeholder collaboration. However, the
authors emphasize blockchain is an early-stage, evolving technology (most reviewed
studies are conceptual rather than empirical) and is not a one-size-fits-all solution;
adoption is situation-specific and must coexist with legacy IT systems. The paper does
not validate these claims empirically itself (it is a synthesis of others' conceptual
work) and explicitly calls for future empirical research, posing open questions
including: which accounting transactions should be recorded on-chain and at what cost,
how blockchain data can be synchronized with AI-enabled auditing, what data standards
blockchain and AI need (and whether XBRL suffices), how blockchain-based accounting
systems should be governed and regulated, and how adoption challenges differ between
large firms and SMPs.
