## Overview

This paper (Werder, Ramesh, and Zhang, 2022, published in ACM Transactions on Management Information Systems) addresses how data provenance—a record describing the origins and processing of data—can be leveraged to support "responsible AI," operationalized through four characteristics: fairness, accountability, transparency, and explainability (FATE). Unlike most responsible-AI research, which focuses on algorithmic bias, the paper centers on biases introduced before the algorithm ever sees the data: at the point of data collection (origins) and during data pre-processing. It identifies five categories of bias from data origins (population data, measurement error, data quality chasm, data repurposing, data augmentation) and five from pre-processing (dataset shifts, opaque pre-processing, data labeling, adversarial manipulation, transfer learning), mapping each to its effects on FATE. Building on this taxonomy, the paper proposes a three-pronged data provenance framework—organizational data governance, data traceability (including blockchain), and technological advances such as explainable AI (XAI)—and illustrates the framework with two real-world failure cases (the EPIC sepsis prediction model and Amazon's AI recruiting tool). It closes with a four-topic research agenda (conceptual clarity, resolving tradeoffs, AI ethics, and designing responsible AI) intended to guide future scholarship on data provenance for responsible AI.

## Background

The paper situates itself within the broader AI ethics literature, noting that scholars have proposed various high-level ethical principles for AI governance without reaching universal consensus (citing Fjeld et al. 2020; Jobin, Ienca, and Vayena 2019; Zhu et al. 2022), and that fairness, accountability, and transparency have received particular attention as a research cluster (citing Jobin et al. 2019; Friedler and Wilson 2018). It draws on the explainable AI (XAI) literature as a complementary strand, citing Gunning et al. (2019) for the emergence of XAI and McDermid et al. (2021) for work bridging technical and ethical considerations. The formal definition of data provenance used throughout is taken from the PROV Data Model (Belhajjame et al. 2013) and earlier database provenance work (Buneman, Khanna, and Wang-Chiew 2001; Cheney, Chiticariu, and Tan 2007), with traceability framed via Ramesh and Jarke's (2001) requirements traceability work. The paper cites real-world disaster cases as motivation for responsible AI, including incorrect patient treatment, exacerbated poverty (Macaulay 2020), wrongful arrest, and unjust criminal sentencing, alongside calls for action from the ACM US Technology Policy Committee (2020) and the Coalition for Critical Technology (2020). It draws on prior empirical findings about specific bias mechanisms: shortcut learning and underspecification in deep learning (Geirhos et al. 2020; D'Amour et al. 2020), confounded hip-fracture prediction models (Badgeley et al. 2019), data labeling performed by underqualified "ghost workers" (Murali 2019), GAN-based data augmentation for liver lesion classification (Frid-Adar et al. 2018), and noise-handling and fairness-in-subset-selection techniques (Gupta and Gupta 2019; Mehrotra and Celis 2021). For organizational governance, it references data governance frameworks (Janssen et al. 2020), datasheets for datasets (Gebru et al. 2021), and the end-to-end provenance project tooling (Ellison et al. 2020). Blockchain-based provenance architectures—ProvChain and LineageChain (Liang et al. 2017)—and applications to "dark data" (Neha and Pahwa 2020) are cited as existing technical approaches to traceability. The "responsibility gap" concept is drawn from Matthias (2004) and Johnson (2015), and the human-in-the-loop debiasing approach is attributed to Hsu (2020). The two illustrative cases—EPIC's sepsis prediction model and Amazon's AI recruiting tool—are based on external evaluations and journalism (Wong et al. 2021; Bennett et al. 2019; Dastin 2018; Long, Seattle Times).

## Key Points

- The paper introduces a taxonomy of ten data-related bias categories distinct from algorithmic bias: five originating from data sources (population data mismatch, measurement error, data quality chasm, data repurposing, data augmentation) and five from data pre-processing (dataset shifts, opaque pre-processing, data labeling, adversarial manipulation, transfer learning).
- It maps each of these ten bias categories to specific effects on the four FATE characteristics (fairness, accountability, transparency, explainability), summarized in a structured table, providing a reference for diagnosing which provenance gaps threaten which responsible-AI property.
- The paper argues that AI outputs are increasingly chained as inputs to other AI systems, so undetected data-origin biases can propagate and compound across systems (e.g., a tumor classification feeding into a readmission risk score).
- It proposes a three-part data provenance framework for responsible AI: (1) establishing organizational data governance (managing metadata via cataloging/curation, and conducting data audits combining data profiling and impact analysis), (2) demanding data traceability (guiding data acquisition practices and leveraging blockchain-based provenance, citing ProvChain and LineageChain), and (3) leveraging technological advances for data provenance (XAI methods such as LIME, LORE, Anchor, and layer-wise relevance propagation; noise-handling and GAN-based denoising techniques; and self-supervised "pre-text task" methods for discovering inherent data structure).
- The paper claims data provenance and XAI are complementary rather than redundant: XAI addresses algorithmic opaqueness, while data provenance addresses data opaqueness, and combining both yields more complete transparency and explainability.
- It identifies an inherent tension between data privacy (e.g., GDPR) and data lineage/traceability, recommending privacy-preserving approaches such as federated learning to reconcile the two.
- Using the EPIC sepsis prediction model as a case study, the paper claims that data repurposing (billing codes used as a proxy for clinical sepsis), population bias, transfer learning failure across hospital systems, and dataset shift (changing clinical practices) jointly explain the model's poor real-world performance and that organizational data audits combined with XAI could have surfaced these issues earlier.
- Using the Amazon AI recruiting tool as a second case study, the paper claims that a dataset shift (outdated 10-year hiring data reflecting historical gender imbalance) and population bias produced unfair, gender-biased candidate screening, and that a data audit establishing provenance could have detected this before deployment.
- The paper claims existing organizational practice treats data provenance as a compliance cost driven by regulation (HIPAA, GDPR), whereas it should instead be treated as a strategic, dynamic, lifecycle-spanning investment that benefits multiple projects reusing the same data sources.
- It proposes a four-area research agenda for data provenance and responsible AI: (1) achieving conceptual clarity around overlapping terms (e.g., explainability vs. interpretability, data lineage vs. data pedigree vs. data provenance) via taxonomy development; (2) resolving tradeoffs among FATE goals (e.g., privacy vs. traceability, accuracy vs. interpretability) using multi-criteria decision-making methods such as conjoint analysis and AHP; (3) addressing AI ethics questions including regional legal/moral variation and the "responsibility gap"; and (4) designing responsible AI systems that bridge technical explainability solutions with user-level interpretability.

## Conclusion

The paper concludes that data provenance is a necessary but currently underutilized mechanism for mitigating data-originated biases and advancing FATE in AI-based systems, and that the loss of provenance information at any point in a data's lifecycle propagates forward, undermining all downstream uses. Its central claim—that focusing on data origins and pre-processing (rather than solely on algorithms) reveals an important and largely overlooked source of responsible-AI failures—is supported through the conceptual bias taxonomy and reinforced by the two real-world case studies (EPIC sepsis model, Amazon recruiting tool), both of which the authors argue exhibit multiple of their identified bias categories and could have been mitigated by their proposed governance/traceability/XAI framework. However, the paper is primarily conceptual and normative: it does not empirically test the proposed framework's effectiveness, quantify costs/benefits of implementing data provenance, or provide implementation-level technical specifications. The authors explicitly flag this as an open area, noting that organizations under budget/schedule pressure may neglect provenance investments or engage in "virtue washing." They identify several open research questions as future work: developing a clearer nomological network distinguishing related concepts (explainability vs. interpretability, data lineage vs. provenance vs. pedigree); empirically resolving tradeoffs among FATE characteristics (e.g., the explainability–fairness and accuracy–interpretability tradeoffs) using multi-criteria decision methods; understanding how organizational and regional/regulatory context (GDPR, HIPAA) shapes responsible AI design and the "responsibility gap"; and developing design guidelines that connect technical XAI/provenance mechanisms to actual user interpretability. The paper frames data provenance for responsible AI as an emerging, cross-disciplinary research area requiring further empirical validation, tooling, and policy development.
