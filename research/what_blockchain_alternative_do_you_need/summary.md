## Overview

This ESORICS 2018 CBT workshop paper by Tommy Koens and Erik Poll (Radboud University) addresses the proliferation of "decision schemes" intended to help organizations determine whether—and which type of—blockchain to adopt for a given use case, a question that gained urgency amid an estimated US$2 billion in projected 2018 global blockchain spending and over 1000 filed patents. The authors rigorously analyze 30 existing decision schemes drawn from academia, industry, and the web, classifying them into three models (Model 1: should you use blockchain; Model 2: which blockchain type; Model 3: which alternative technology) and classifying their constituent questions into five categories. Their analysis surfaces two main problems: concrete contradictions where different schemes give different answers to identical inputs, and a deeper structural flaw that most schemes are biased toward blockchain because they ignore non-blockchain alternatives (central databases, distributed databases, and non-blockchain distributed ledgers such as Ripple and Corda). To remedy this, they propose a new flow-diagram decision scheme built only from the 9 questions that genuinely differentiate between technologies, which explicitly routes users to alternative technologies and admits only one blockchain end state—the "classic" public permissionless blockchain. They argue this single scheme can replace all 30 and serve as a sanity check for blockchain initiatives.

## Background

The paper builds on a substantial body of prior critique and terminology work it cites in support of its argument. It cites Nakamoto's 2008 Bitcoin paper, noting that the term "blockchain" never appears there (Nakamoto uses "distributed time-stamp server"), and Perlman (2017) for the framing that blockchain's distinctive property is achieving consensus among a consortium of unknown participants. On terminology confusion, it leans on Birch et al. and Maull et al., who likewise observe that authors use "blockchain" inconsistently, and on Narayanan, who argues a "private blockchain" is just a confusing name for a shared database. On blockchain's mutability (countering "immutability" claims by Peters & Panayi, Pilkington, and Tapscott & Tapscott), it cites Bonneau et al. on rewriting history with majority hash power ("mutable-by-hashing-power"), Eyal & Sirer ("Majority is not enough") showing roughly a quarter of resources may suffice, and the Ethereum DAO hard fork as a real-world example of rewriting history. For alternatives lacking blockchain's limitations it cites Ripple's consensus protocol (claiming ~50,000 tps vs Bitcoin's 7 and Ethereum's 14), R3 Corda (a decentralized/distributed ledger heavily inspired by but not using a chain of blocks), Storj (peer-to-peer cloud storage), and Omniledger for scalability research. Crucially, the paper situates itself among many prior voices (Greenspan, Buitenhek, Carter & Ubacht, Gerard's "Attack of the 50 Foot Blockchain," Pisa & Juden, Perlman, Peck, and others) who argue blockchain is unnecessary or unsuitable for many proposed applications. The 30 analyzed schemes themselves (e.g., Wüst & Gervais, Hyperledger, IBM, Peck, Pahl, Lin et al., Greenspan, Deloitte) are the primary material the paper draws on and critiques.

## Key Points

- The authors collected and analyzed all 30 blockchain decision schemes they could find in literature and on the web; 25 are flow diagrams ("F") and 5 are questionnaires ("Q").
- All 30 schemes can be classified into three models by the question they answer: Model 1 (should you use blockchain), Model 2 (which blockchain type), and Model 3 (which alternative technology), and many schemes combine models. The number of end states varies widely (from 1 to 7) even among schemes of the same type.
- The schemes use the term "blockchain" arbitrarily and inconsistently, with varying granularity of end-state descriptions (e.g., it is unclear whether "public BC" means permissionless or permissioned); the authors propose their own classification of database technologies: central databases (central ledgers, shared ledgers) vs. distributed databases (distributed ledgers, with blockchain as the special case of unknown participants).
- The authors demonstrate concrete contradictions between schemes: (1) Cooke vs. Gardner — identical answers (need a database, multiple writers, writers trusted) yield "undetermined" from Cooke but "you don't need a blockchain" from Gardner, due to Cooke's extra "are writers' interests unified?" question; (2) Wüst vs. Hyperledger — same answers lead to differently-named end states because Wüst is more fine-grained (two axes) than Hyperledger (one axis); (3) IBM vs. Verslype — IBM says complex business logic argues for blockchain while Verslype says simple business rules do, a direct contradiction likely from assuming different blockchain types (Ethereum-with-smart-contracts vs. Bitcoin).
- The authors classify all scheme questions into five labels: T (tech-type, differentiates technologies), L (current blockchain limitations), E (system-design/prerequisite), P (process), and D (the two meta-questions the scheme aims to answer). They find most questions are process ("P") questions and that 25 of 30 schemes contain questions that do not contribute to the scheme's goal.
- Only 9 questions genuinely differentiate between technologies (e.g., need a database, can you use a TTP, shared write access, participants known, need to control functionality, public transactions, transaction interaction, high transaction throughput, store large transactional data); none of the 30 schemes address all of these.
- The authors argue the "are writers' interests unified?" question (used by Cooke, Suichies, WEF) is invalid because one must always assume a malicious participant whose interests are misaligned (the double-spend problem must be designed for by default).
- The proposed new scheme (Figure 2) routes through 9 sequential questions to 8 end states: no DB needed; central DB (single writer); shared central DB (multiple writers needing controlled functionality or a trusted third party); distributed DB (e.g., Storj, no transaction interaction); distributed ledger for known participants where anyone can join (e.g., Ripple); distributed ledger with access control (e.g., Corda); "currently no solution available" (when high throughput >~2000 tps or large >Terabyte data storage is required); and public permissionless blockchain (unknown participants, throughput/storage not critical).
- The authors argue that only the "classic" public permissionless blockchain truly makes sense as a blockchain, because public/private permissioned "blockchains" are essentially shared databases, and in any scenario where a permissioned blockchain could work, an alternative technology without blockchain's drawbacks is always a better choice ("Blockchains are often sufficient but not often necessary").
- The authors assert that decision schemes which omit alternative technologies are "incomplete and hence wrong," and that their single scheme can replace all 30 because it includes all relevant differentiating questions plus alternative-technology end states.

## Conclusion

The paper concludes that its central claims are supported by the analysis: over half of the 30 schemes contain questions that do not contribute to the scheme's goal, many schemes are biased in favor of blockchain because their end states only include blockchain variants, and demonstrable inconsistencies exist where identical decisions yield different outcomes (or opposite decisions yield similar outcomes). The authors confirm a "Babylonian confusion" around the term blockchain (echoing Birch et al. and Maull), which motivated their preliminary terminology framework. Their core recommendation is that decision schemes must include alternative technologies that provide the desired functionality without blockchain's limitations (poor transaction scalability, unsuitability for storing large data, and the practical difficulty—but not impossibility—of altering history), and that if blockchain is used, only a public permissionless blockchain is genuinely warranted. The authors note their scheme is limited to the technical perspective and identify several open research directions: extending it with non-technical drivers of adoption (philosophical beliefs, economic incentives), incorporating more distributed ledger technologies and more of their current issues, further analyzing the degree of consensus among existing schemes, and—most notably—deeper study of the concept of trust, which their scheme largely sets aside (touching it only via "can you use a third party?"), since introducing blockchain shifts and may even introduce new types of trust rather than eliminating the need for it.
