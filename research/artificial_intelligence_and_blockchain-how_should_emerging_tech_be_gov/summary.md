## Overview

This policy review by Cornelius Kalenzi (Frontiers in Research Metrics and Analytics, 2022) surveys the emerging landscape of AI and blockchain governance by mapping the ecosystem of platforms and actors across the public sector, private industry, and civil society. The paper addresses the governance question raised by the rapid, disruptive adoption of Fourth Industrial Revolution (4IR) technologies—privacy breaches, fraud, bias, surveillance, technology-related unemployment, and AI-powered military and political risks—and the resulting "Pandora's Box" of legal, ethical, and regulatory issues. Its core contribution is an ecosystem map (presented as Table 1 and Figures 1–5) identifying major stakeholders, their roles, and their underlying motivations (e.g., profit for Big Tech, mission/human rights for NGOs, public safety for regulators), and an analysis of where these motivations converge and diverge. Rather than proposing a single governance model, the paper argues that governance outcomes will be shaped by the relative power struggles between these platforms, and recommends that jurisdictions re-examine and update existing legal/institutional frameworks where possible, while developing new "agile governance" regimes for cases where existing law is fundamentally inadequate (e.g., AI used to influence elections, crypto fraud, autonomous vehicles). It also typologizes national governance approaches into market-centered (self-regulation), comprehensive/strong (regulation-led), and soft-law/middle-ground regimes.

## Background

The paper situates itself within a broader 2017-onward debate (citing Pagallo, 2018; Winfield and Jirotka, 2018) on governing digital technologies, noting that civil society and intergovernmental bodies have published over 200 AI principles and that more than 450 regulatory proposals targeting digital innovation have been issued globally (over 200 in Europe alone, per Hogan Lovells, 2019). It draws on prior governance-framework proposals: Winfield and Jirotka (2018), who show how ethics feeds into standards and then regulation; Cath (2018) on governance of high-risk technologies; Etzioni (2017), whose three-rule framework for AI regulation (subject AI to updated existing laws, require AI to disclose it is not human, and prohibit unauthorized retention/publication of personal data) is cited directly; Takanashi et al. (2020) on multi-stakeholder governance mechanisms for blockchain-based finance; and Feijóo et al. (2020) on "technology diplomacy." It also leans on Cusumano et al. (2021) for the claim that most governments lack the skills and resources to regulate fast-moving digital platforms, and on Kwoka and Valletti (2021) for the opposing view that Big Tech will not self-regulate without a strong government hand. The paper references the "ethics washing" critique developed by Wagner (2018), Benkler (2019), and Peukert and Kloker (2020), as well as Jobin et al. (2019) on the global landscape of AI ethics guidelines. Concrete policy and institutional context drawn from elsewhere includes the OECD AI Principles (Budish and Gasser, 2019), the EU's GDPR (Albrecht, 2016) and proposed Artificial Intelligence Act, China's New Generation AI Development Plan (~$150 billion), the US Senate's $250 billion tech bill (Franck, 2021), the EU's NextGenerationEU investment (€806.9 billion), South Korea's Digital New Deal (Yonhap News, 2020), the European Blockchain Partnership and Horizon 2020 blockchain funding, ISO/IEC JTC 1/SC 42 and IEEE P7004/P7005/P7006 standards, the Partnership on AI and Blockchain Association as industry self-regulatory bodies, and case examples such as Google's facial-recognition standards work with the ITU (Gross et al., 2019), the firing of Google's AI ethics team (Karen, 2020), NOYB's role in invalidating the EU-US Safe Harbor agreement, and the Binance/crypto-fraud regulatory gaps (Wall Street Journal; BBC, 2021a, 2021b).

## Key Points

- The paper constructs an "emerging technology governance ecosystem map" (Table 1) categorizing stakeholders into three levels—market/industry (Big Tech, entrepreneurs, consumers, investors), civil society (standards bodies, NGOs, activist academics, media/non-profits), and the public sector (regulators, innovation investors, governance councils, multinational bodies, advisory councils)—each with distinct roles and motivations.
- It argues that industry-centered governance (exemplified by the US) prioritizes deployment and profit over ethics ("business first, ethics second"), citing examples such as discriminatory AI smart speakers, fraudulent ICOs, and Amazon/Google's continuation of contentious projects (facial recognition, Pentagon contracts) despite employee and stakeholder objections.
- It claims that industry self-regulation efforts—ethics committees, published AI principles, and bodies like the Partnership on AI and the Blockchain Association—function largely as "ethics washing" and lobbying platforms with no binding mechanisms, auditing, or enforcement power.
- It contends that tech firms (particularly Chinese AI firms like ZTE and Zahua) are shaping international standards (e.g., via the ITU) in ways that grant them outsized influence over technology policy in developing countries, with minimal civil society oversight.
- It identifies civil society (ISO, WEF, IEEE, NGOs, media) as playing advocacy, watchdog, and quasi-regulatory roles—publishing over 100 AI principles and standards (e.g., ISO/IEC JTC 1/SC 42, IEEE P7004/P7005/P7006)—but argues these efforts have limited enforceable power and suffer from an "ethics and governance divide" that privileges wealthy countries (e.g., the "right to be forgotten" applying only in Europe).
- It documents that national governments have reached broad consensus on promoting AI and blockchain as economic growth engines (citing major investment programs in China, the US, EU, South Korea, Singapore, and Australia) but diverge sharply on regulatory approach—ranging from China's outright crypto ban to the EU's GDPR/AI Act to the US's largely self-regulatory, non-binding stance.
- It introduces a threefold typology of national governance regimes: market-centered self-regulation platforms (US, China for tech), comprehensive/strong government platforms (Europe), and soft-law/middle-ground platforms (Singapore, France, Sweden, New Zealand), each driven by different domestic motivations (e.g., Singapore's dependence on Big Tech presence vs. Europe's consumer-protection orientation).
- It claims that multinational AI governance initiatives (OECD AI Principles, Global Partnership on Artificial Intelligence) are effectively "rich nations' clubs" that exclude the Global South from the AI governance debate.
- It argues that despite surface-level convergence among industry, government, and civil society on the need for principles and standards, their underlying motivations (profit vs. mission vs. public safety) diverge enough to undermine a broad governance consensus, and that civil society in particular lacks the "teeth" to translate advocacy into enforceable action.
- It recommends that governments and the private sector leverage purchasing and licensing power (e.g., for facial-recognition and supply-chain projects) to push "ethics by design" adoption, and points to the FDA's Total Product Life Cycle approach for AI/ML medical devices and the UK Centre for Data Ethics and Innovation as models for practical safeguard mechanisms.

## Conclusion

The paper concludes that public, private, and civil society actors are still only beginning to grapple with the governance challenges posed by AI and blockchain, and that no consensus exists on an optimal governance model. While there is broad agreement on the need for innovation-promoting policies and for ethical principles/standards, the paper finds that the differing underlying motivations of firms (profit), governments (variable—innovation vs. protection), and civil society (mission/advocacy without enforcement power) will continue to prevent a unified governance consensus. It predicts divergent regional trajectories: consolidation of self-regulation in Big-Tech-dominant environments (US, China for technology development), consolidation of comprehensive regulatory regimes in consumer-protection-oriented environments (Europe), and continued "wait-and-see" stagnation among most developing countries, which lack the resources and leverage to regulate effectively (per Cusumano et al., 2021)—leaving civil society comparatively powerless unless it evolves toward stronger, certified governance platforms. The paper's central recommendation—rather than a definitive finding—is that jurisdictions should re-examine existing legal/institutional frameworks and amend them where applicable, while developing new "agile governance" regimes for genuinely novel harms (e.g., AI-driven election manipulation, crypto fraud, autonomous vehicles) where existing law is inadequate. The analysis is explicitly framed as preliminary and descriptive rather than empirically tested, and the author flags several open research questions: how governance platforms across public, private, and civil-society sectors can practically collaborate given their differing power positions; whether findings from AI governance research can be transferred to inform blockchain/cryptocurrency governance (and vice versa); and how the "ethics and governance divide" between wealthy and developing nations might be addressed. The paper does not test hypotheses empirically but instead offers a conceptual mapping intended to ground future, more focused research on either AI or blockchain governance individually.
