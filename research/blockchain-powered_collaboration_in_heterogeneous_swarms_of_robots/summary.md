## Overview

This paper (Queralta & Westerlund, University of Turku) proposes a blockchain-based architecture for managing collaboration within heterogeneous, ad hoc swarms of autonomous robots that differ in sensing capabilities and onboard computational resources. The core problem it addresses is how robots can optimize what data (type, size, resolution) they share with each other so as to enhance each robot's situational awareness, without explicitly disclosing or having to trust information about their hardware, sensors, or processing capacity. Its two central contributions are: (1) using Proof of Work (PoW) puzzles, solved periodically by every robot, as an online estimator of each robot's relative available computational resources (faster solving implies more compute, which justifies sending it more data); and (2) using smart contracts plus submitted "data stamps" (samples) to evaluate and rank the quality/accuracy of each robot's sensor data, with trustability established by having other robots operating in the same environment validate those samples against historical stamps. These two parameters (compute estimate and data-quality ranking) feed an optimization problem, executed as a smart contract, that allocates peer-to-peer network bandwidth. The work is framed around collaborative sensing/cooperative mapping (e.g., smart-city self-driving cars and drones), borrows scalability and consensus mechanisms from Ethereum 2.0 (PoS via Casper, sharding, gas congealing, demurrage), and reports only initial proof-of-concept results rather than a full implementation.

## Background

The paper builds heavily on existing blockchain and consensus literature and frames its design against it. It says (citing others) that consensus mechanisms in multi-agent and blockchain systems can provide decentralized, scalable, secure, and trustable solutions to the multi-robot consensus problem, and that integrating blockchain into swarm robotics has been a growing research topic, including managing byzantine robots (Strobel et al.) and using Merkle trees for secure information sharing and mission reporting (Ferrer). It draws on reputation-based distributed task allocation for heterogeneous robots (Shi et al.) and on swarm self-assembly work like the Kilobot project (Rubenstein et al., Groß et al.). For consensus theory it cites Nakamoto's Bitcoin PoW, Dwork et al.'s original PoW-as-spam-filter idea, the Ethereum yellow paper (Wood) and Solidity/EVM smart contracts, and Proof of Stake as first implemented in Nxtcoin and formalized by Bentov et al., plus Ethereum 2.0's Casper PoS with slashing. Critically, its PoW-as-compute-estimation idea is grounded in prior mining-pool work: it cites Eyal et al. on using full and partial proofs of work to quantify and fairly reward miners' computational contributions, and Miller et al.'s opposite "non-outsourceable puzzles." For scalability it cites Luu et al.'s Elastico (first byzantine-tolerant sharding for permissionless blockchains, scaling with computation power) and Kokoris-Kogias et al.'s OmniLedger (linear scaling with validators, sub-two-second confirmations, the Atomix cross-shard lock/unlock protocol). It also leans on Ethereum 2.0 specifications for cross-shard communication via receipts, transparent sharding, gas congealing, and demurrage. The experimental datasets cited are CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky) for CNN classification and KITTI (Geiger et al.) for visual odometry, with lidar data from a prior localization platform.

## Key Points

- Proposes using periodically-solved PoW puzzles as an online, relative estimator of each collaborating robot's *available* (not total) computational resources; faster PoW solving implies more spare compute, so the network sends that robot more data, scaled so as not to overload the receiver.
- Reframes PoW so that the reward for solving is not cryptocurrency but a "data reward" — robots that dedicate more compute to PoW receive more/better data, which removes the incentive to lie about capabilities since under-reporting compute only reduces the data a robot receives.
- Argues partial proofs of work (not just full solutions) should be used so the network can estimate the effort of all robots, not only the one that mines a block, and recommends fixed (not adaptive) PoW difficulty so low-resource robots can still participate.
- Defines a "Proof of Quality" / data-stamp mechanism: rather than putting all shared data on-chain, each data exchange transaction submits a representative data sample (stamp) to the blockchain, evaluated by smart contracts on type, density/resolution, and comparison against historical stamps from the same location.
- Establishes trustability through peer validation: stamps are compared by other robots operating in the same environment, with comparison results (match / match-with-resolution-info / mismatch) recorded on-chain, and proposes a validation graph to detect collusion (fully-connected subgraphs of attackers), noting honest majorities and well-visited locations eventually expose counterfeit data.
- Proposes a hybrid consensus design: Proof of Stake (Casper-style) for transaction validation for scalability, while retaining periodic compulsory PoW solely for compute estimation, with demurrage congealing cryptocurrency so it expires and penalties limiting dishonest nodes' data throughput.
- Uses PoW at join time to set a minimum compute bar and prevent Sybil attacks, and tests link bandwidth/latency on join to estimate peer-to-peer network capacity.
- Formulates data sharing as a constrained optimization (maximizing a weighted sum, with weights alpha/beta, of data usability and data trustability) bounded by per-link bandwidth and receivers' processing power, solved within a smart contract; proposes spatial shards for local rankings plus randomized shards for a global ranking.
- Distinguishes two blockchain genesis models: a single longevous blockchain (best supported by fixed connected infrastructure, e.g., smart-city base stations / 5G) versus ad hoc blockchains created/destroyed on the fly (better suited to single-manufacturer fleets).
- Provides the key empirical claim: PoW hashing power correlates well with CNN classification latency across heterogeneous robot compute platforms (Intel Up, Up Gateway, NVIDIA Jetson TX2, Intel i5-6200U), validating PoW as a compute proxy — but fails when a GPU accelerates the workload (the Jetson TX2 outperformed its hashing-power-predicted classification rate).
- Characterizes lidar data-stamp size as a function of geometry: linear with visible wall length for a building corner and linear with distance for a tree, but nonlinear for a car (because both visible area and projected lidar channel count vary with distance/viewpoint), establishing that stamp comparison must account for viewpoint and distance.

## Conclusion

The paper concludes that blockchain technology can manage two key heterogeneous-collaboration parameters — relative computational resources and data quality — without robots sharing explicit hardware or sensor information, and that connected infrastructure (e.g., smart-city traffic cameras and base stations) could maintain the blockchain and even act as a validating data source. The initial results partially support the central PoW claim: hashing power tracked CNN-classification latency consistently across four robot-grade compute platforms (standard deviation of hashing power below 3%), confirming PoW as a viable compute proxy — with the explicit caveat that single-thread CPU-bound PoW cannot model GPU- or ASIC-accelerated workloads (demonstrated by the Jetson TX2 outlier), implying future work needs multiple PoW schemes for different resource types and strategies to resist specialized accelerators. The lidar experiments support the feasibility of distance/viewpoint-aware data characterization. The authors are candid that the approach is immature: several enabling blockchain technologies (sharding with smart contracts, Casper PoS, gas congealing) are not yet production-ready, so a full efficient implementation is not currently viable, and the initial Ethereum prototype only attaches data samples as transaction payloads (with gas growing with payload size) rather than implementing the smart-contract data ranking. Open challenges flagged for future research include: managing blockchain lifecycles and trust anchoring; preventing broad-scale collusion attacks where coalitions submit and mutually confirm counterfeit data stamps; and the hardest problem — preventing robots from withholding their true data quality (e.g., deliberately downsampling point clouds or blurring images) to free-ride, since peers cannot easily verify a robot's true sensing capability and penalizing low-quality providers would unfairly harm genuinely resource-constrained robots like drones. Future work promises deeper analysis of PoW puzzle types, the two genesis models, and partial implementation/testing in concrete application scenarios.
