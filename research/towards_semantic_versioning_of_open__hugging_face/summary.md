## Overview

This paper presents the first large-scale empirical study of release, naming, and versioning practices for pre-trained language models (PTLMs) hosted on Hugging Face (HF), motivated by the absence of any standardized semantic versioning mechanism for open ML model registries comparable to the major.minor.patch conventions used in traditional software ecosystems. Using a mixed-methods approach (manual card sorting, regex-based extraction, statistical testing) over a curated dataset of 52,227 PTLMs derived from an initial pool of 196,211 NLP models on HF (as of March 17, 2024), the authors address three research questions covering (1) naming and versioning conventions, (2) reproducibility/transparency of model variants, and (3) the alignment between major/minor version identifiers and the actual changes between releases. The study finds 148 distinct naming conventions composed of 12 segment types, that only 6.64% of models specify a version identifier (predominantly "major" style v1/v2), that 40.87% of changes to model weight files go entirely undocumented in names or version tags, that the 52,227 PTLMs trace back to only 299 base models (dominated by Llama, Bert, and Mistral), and that there is no statistically significant difference in the types of changes accompanying major versus minor version bumps—suggesting current versioning is largely arbitrary. The paper's broader contribution is a discussion of why one-dimensional semantic versioning (as used for code) does not map cleanly onto the multidimensional nature of PTLMs, and a set of concrete recommendations (standardized naming segments, semantic version calculators, SBOM-inspired provenance metadata) for moving the ecosystem toward more formal versioning.

## Background

- The paper grounds its motivation in the well-established value of semantic versioning in traditional software engineering, citing work showing it helps automate dependency management and avoid unnecessary integration work (Lam et al., 2020), addresses inconsistent breaking changes (Raemaekers et al., 2017), supports continuous delivery (Carvalho and Seco, 2021), improves cross-ecosystem policy compliance (Decan and Mens, 2019), and reduces the impact of breaking changes on client code (Ruhroth et al., 2014).
- It situates PTLMs within the broader rise of pre-trained models, citing foundational works such as BERT (Devlin et al., 2018), GPT (OpenAI, 2023), RoBERTa (Liu et al., 2019), Llama (Touvron et al., 2023), Mistral (Jiang et al., 2023a), and Gemma (Team et al., 2024), and adopts a 1-million-parameter threshold for "PTLM" based on Eldan and Li (2023), who showed even small models can exhibit reasoning capability.
- The paper relies heavily on prior naming-convention research by Jiang et al. (2023b), who surveyed HF naming practices and trained a tool to detect base-model naming anomalies with 92.18% accuracy; this paper explicitly differentiates its "naming conventions" (combinations of segments) from Jiang et al.'s "naming segments" (individual components).
- The paper draws on prior documentation/transparency studies, including Oreamuno et al. (2024) (model/dataset documentation gaps across 55,280 HF models), Taraghi et al. (2024) (model card prevalence across 239,422 models), Mitchell et al. (2019) (the "model cards" framework), Toma and Bezemer (2024) (dataset/model versioning practices in ML applications on GitHub), Pepe et al. (2024) (dataset/bias/license documentation on HF), and Bhat et al. (2023) (the DocML tool for documentation traceability).
- Other cited HF ecosystem studies include Castaño et al. (2024) on ML model evolution/maintenance on HF, Castaño et al. (2023) on HF's carbon-emission reporting, Kathikar et al. (2023) on vulnerabilities across 110,000 HF repos, and Jiang et al. (2024b)'s PeaTMOSS dataset for pre-trained model supply-chain analysis.
- For modification-method definitions, the paper cites prior technical work on fine-tuning (Min et al., 2017; Severyn and Moschitti, 2015), deduplication (Kandpal et al., 2022), knowledge distillation (Sun et al., 2019), quantization (Jacob et al., 2018), pruning (Zhu and Gupta, 2017), and parameter-efficient tuning/adapters (Houlsby et al., 2019).
- For the semantic versioning discussion, the paper references Preston-Werner's SemVer 2.0.0 specification and Lam et al.'s (2020) proposal of "semantic version calculators" based on formal contracts, as well as software bill of materials (SBOM) concepts (Xia et al., 2023) as a possible model for decoupling provenance/versioning metadata.
- The paper also draws analogies to known software supply-chain incidents — the npm left-pad incident and the Therac-25 radiation therapy software failure — to argue for the practical risks of poor versioning discipline.

## Key Points

- This paper provides the first comprehensive empirical characterization of PTLM release practices on Hugging Face, covering naming, versioning, reproducibility, and transparency across 52,227 models.
- It identifies 12 distinct naming-segment types (identifier, base model, size, description, variant type, version, training mechanism, task, dataset, language, others, creation date) and 148 distinct combinations of these segments forming naming conventions, with {identifier}, {identifier}{size}, and {base model}{identifier} the most common.
- Only 6.64% (3,471 of 52,227) of PTLMs include any version identifier in their name, and of those, 67% follow a "major" versioning style (v1, v2, ...) versus 33% "minor" style (v1.x).
- Models whose names mention size have download rates over 11x higher than those mentioning only the base model (1,163.21 vs. 101.88 average downloads), while model name length shows no significant correlation with download rate (Pearson r = -0.024, R² = 0.0006).
- Across 1,282,874 file changes observed in repository commits, 40.87% are to model binary/weight files, yet only 0.66% of these changes are reflected via version identifiers in model names — i.e., roughly 524,419 implicit "versions" exist versus 3,471 explicitly declared ones.
- Security-focused tensor files (.safetensors) show the highest change frequency among model binary formats (62.63% of changes, 7.88 changes/model on average), while PyTorch model files show the highest average changes per model (31.92) despite lower overall adoption.
- Since 2022, all 52,227 studied PTLMs derive from just 299 base models, with the top 15 (Llama, Bert, Mistral, Distilbert, GPT2, T5, RoBERTa, XLM-RoBERTa, Gemma, Mixtral, Bart, GPT-NeoX, Marian, mT5, Phi) accounting for 85.80% of releases; normalized by model age, Gemma (47.9%) and Mistral (36.9%) show the fastest growth rates relative to Llama.
- 98% of PTLMs have configuration files identifying their base model/provenance, but only 0.085% of configuration files explicitly state the variant type.
- 70.72% of PTLM releases do not specify their variant type at all; among those that do (15,287 releases), Quantized (69.3%) and Fine-tuned (29.6%) dominate, with Deduped and Distilled each under 1%.
- The paper identifies and categorizes 14 distinct variant-type keyword indicators (e.g., "ft"/"finetuned", "4bit"/"8bit"/"int4"/"int8"/"awq"/"QAT"/"ptq"/"float16", "deduped", "distilled") into four variant-type categories: Fine-tuning, Quantization, Deduplication, and Knowledge Distillation.
- Llama and Mistral account for 85.65% of quantized PTLMs; Bert and Distilbert account for 52.93% of fine-tuned and 76.05% of distilled PTLMs; GPT-NeoX accounts for 91.66% of deduplicated PTLMs.
- Only 24% of PTLMs with dataset metadata (8,157 of 33,964) explicitly specify their training dataset via the HF API; of the remaining models lacking such metadata, only 12% mention a dataset name in their model card and only 2% provide a source link.
- 33% of the 52,227 PTLMs were released without any model card, with significant variation by variant type (Deduped 82.7% have cards vs. Fine-tuned only 68.6%), and chi-square tests show statistically significant associations between variant type and both dataset availability (fine-tuned, distilled, quantized) and model card presence (fine-tuned, quantized).
- Mapping predecessor/successor pairs for versioned models, 57% of sampled major versions and 65% of sampled minor versions lack a discoverable predecessor or successor in the same owner's repository, indicating fragmented, multi-repository versioning practices.
- Among predecessor-successor pairs, model cards change most often (71% of major, 80% of minor version pairs); only changes to the base model and to model weight files are statistically significant between major and minor versions (base model p<0.05; weight files p<0.05, odds ratio 2.24).
- The paper catalogs 28 distinct types of changes across 9 categories (configuration, model architecture, license, performance, dataset, training library, energy consumption, performance metrics, other) occurring between versions; major versions exhibit all 28 change types (averaging ~31 changes) while minor versions exhibit only 8 (averaging ~10 changes), and the difference between major/minor is statistically significant only for configuration, license, and "other" change categories (p<0.05).
- Phi-coefficient analysis reveals a very strong association between configuration changes and performance changes (φ=0.46), and several strong/moderate associations (e.g., evaluation metrics–dataset changes φ=0.24, training library–performance changes φ=0.15, model architecture–configuration changes φ=0.15).
- The paper proposes a definition of a "model release" as a registry entry with a meaningful name, unambiguous version identifiers, and all essential artifacts (weights, configs, documentation) plus provenance links to datasets and prior releases.
- It argues that traditional one-dimensional SemVer (X.Y.Z) cannot directly capture PTLM evolution because PTLMs lack a clear notion of "backward compatibility" and are inherently multidimensional (architecture, size, training data, domain), and proposes that any future versioning scheme should encode at minimum identifier, base model, model size, and training mechanism.
- The authors release a public dataset and extraction scripts (Ajibode, 2024) to support replication and further research.

## Conclusion

The study's findings broadly support its motivating concern: PTLM release practices on Hugging Face are highly heterogeneous, under-documented, and largely disconnected from any coherent versioning logic. The 148 naming conventions and low (6.64%) rate of explicit versioning confirm a lack of standardization; the huge gap between declared (3,471) and implicit (524,419) versions confirms that most substantive changes go uncommunicated; and the absence of statistically significant differences between major and minor version changes (except for configuration, license, and "other" categories) supports the hypothesis that major/minor identifiers are often chosen arbitrarily rather than reflecting backward-compatibility semantics as in traditional SemVer. Transparency gaps are also confirmed: 70.72% of releases omit variant-type information and 76% omit training-dataset information, despite 98% having configuration files. The paper does not claim these issues are unique to popular or unpopular models — it explicitly flags as an open question whether high-impact model families (Llama, Qwen, DeepSeek) follow different (better or worse) practices than the long tail, and recommends future work to investigate this. Other noted limitations/threats to validity include: restriction to NLP models (excluding vision, audio, multimodal, tabular, RL models), reliance on HF-specific naming/versioning conventions that may not generalize to other registries (PyTorch Hub, ONNX, Model Zoo), use of file-size comparisons rather than checksums to detect weight-file changes (potentially missing subtle modifications), and incomplete predecessor/successor chains due to deleted or renamed repositories (0.2% of the dataset became inaccessible during the study). The paper's central forward-looking conclusion is that future research should focus on establishing formal semantic versioning practices for PTLMs — including defining what "compatibility" means for models, building semantic version calculators analogous to those proposed for software (Lam et al., 2020), introducing structured, decoupled version/provenance metadata (potentially SBOM-inspired), and standardizing a minimal set of naming segments (identifier, base model, size, training mechanism) as a near-term stopgap while richer metadata infrastructure is developed.
