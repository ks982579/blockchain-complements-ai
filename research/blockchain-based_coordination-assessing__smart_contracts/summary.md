## Overview

This paper (Ciatto, Mariani, Maffi, and Omicini, published in *Information*, 2020) assesses the *coordination* expressive power of mainstream blockchain smart contracts (SC) — that is, their ability to govern and mediate interaction among parties — as distinct from the *computational* expressiveness (Turing-equivalence, termination) that prior literature has emphasized. The authors argue that although SC are commonly used to mediate interaction among mutually untrusted parties, no prior work had evaluated SC along the "interaction dimension." Using the established coordination-models methodology of encoding a model of unknown expressiveness into one of known expressiveness, they adopt the archetypal LINDA tuple-based coordination model as a benchmark and implement it on top of four BCT: Ethereum (two variants), HyperLedger Fabric (HLF), R3 Corda, and Tenderfone (the authors' own custom blockchain). They find that the three mainstream BCT cannot fully support LINDA's suspensive semantics — and therefore cannot achieve full data-driven synchronization — with the limitations being most acute for coordination *between* smart contracts (inter-SC), while Corda additionally breaks reference uncoupling. Tenderfone, which provides "pro-active" agent-oriented SC that communicate via asynchronous message-passing, is the only one of the four to faithfully encode LINDA with all its properties, thereby reaching maximal coordination expressiveness.

## Background

The paper builds on the field of *coordination models and languages* (citing Ciancarini, Gelernter and Carriero, Malone and Crowston), framing coordination as managing dependencies between activities and as the "glue" binding separate activities into an ensemble. It draws on the standard expressiveness-assessment technique from this field — encoding a model of unknown expressiveness into a model of known expressiveness to see which properties are preserved. The benchmark, LINDA (Gelernter), is described as the archetypal *tuple-based coordination* model, valued for its expressiveness, openness, and minimality relative to message-passing; the paper cites prior work studying LINDA's formal expressiveness from multiple perspectives. It situates SC historically as introduced by Ethereum following Szabo's vision, now present in most BCT, and notes the blockchain's recurring core roles across domains (identity management and asset tracking) with examples drawn from cited work on supply chain, healthcare/EMR auditing, and real estate. For each platform it relies on the originating technical descriptions (Ethereum's gas model and PoW; HLF's *execute-order-validate* architecture and endorsement policies; Corda's UTXO/Vault/Flow privacy-first design and Bitcoin lineage; Tendermint's byzantine-fault-tolerant state-machine replication underlying Tenderfone). The paper's own prior conference papers supply the Ethereum and HLF LINDA implementations. In Related Works it positions itself against other blockchain-coordination efforts: ADICO/Grammar-of-Institutions-to-Solidity mapping, Vickrey-auction energy-market SC, blockchain-as-software-connector, BPMN-choreography-to-Solidity (via Petri-net translation), and multi-agent shared-plan execution using template/satellite SC with oracles — noting that most lack full implementations and, critically, do not clarify how participants suspend/wait on task completion rather than poll.

## Key Points

- The paper introduces and operationalizes the "interaction dimension" (coordination expressiveness) as a metric for smart contracts, distinct from and complementary to computational expressiveness — a gap the authors claim was previously unaddressed in the literature.
- It uses the LINDA coordination model as the expressiveness benchmark. LINDA's core elements are *tuples* (data chunks in a tuple language), *templates* (partial-pattern blueprints in a template language), *tuple spaces* (repositories where tuples are put/inspected/withdrawn), and three communication primitives: `out` (put a tuple), `rd` (read/inspect a matching tuple), and `in` (withdraw a matching tuple).
- LINDA's defining features are *suspensive semantics* (`rd`/`in` block until a matching tuple appears, enabling data-driven synchronization), *generative communication* (tuples have a lifecycle independent of their producer), and *associative access* (retrieval by content/structure via template matching). Together these yield synchronization plus reference, space, and time uncoupling. The authors adopt satisfaction of these requirements as the expressiveness metric.
- On Ethereum, LINDA maps tuple spaces to SC, tuples/templates to raw strings (matching = string inclusion), invocations to method calls, and completions to publish–subscribe; coordinated entities are off-chain users only. Ethereum supports generative communication and associative access but *cannot* fully support suspensive semantics because the EVM forbids long/infinite computations (no polling), forces synchronous inter-SC calls, and offers no way to postpone/resume computation.
- On HLF, LINDA maps tuple spaces to chaincodes, completions to the Event Service; despite the *execute-order-validate* architecture technically allowing long computations, chaincodes still cannot busy-wait, can only interact synchronously, and cannot suspend/schedule/postpone — so suspensive semantics is again only partially supported (HLF does gain access-control and parallelism/scalability advantages via endorsement policies).
- On Corda, tuples map to States, tuple spaces to Vaults, invocations/completions to Flows; coordinated entities are Corda nodes. Corda has the *least* coordination expressiveness: it shares the suspensive-semantics limitation and *additionally breaks reference uncoupling*, because its privacy-by-default design requires a publishing node to know the identifiers of all nodes meant to perceive a shared State.
- Tenderfone is the authors' custom, permissioned, currency-less BCT (built on Tendermint) whose key innovation is the *pro-active smart contract*: SC have a belief base and plan base expressed in First-Order Logic (Prolog), encapsulate data, behavior, and control (modeled as agents), and interact *asynchronously* via message-passing with both off-chain users and other SC.
- The LINDA-on-Tenderfone implementation (given in full Prolog in the paper) maps tuples/templates to FOL terms, matching to *logic unification* (more expressive than string matching), and both primitive invocations *and completions* to asynchronous messages. This is the only implementation where primitive completions can target *other smart contracts* while remaining compatible with suspensive semantics, enabling true inter-SC coordination.
- Tenderfone is the only one of the four BCT to achieve maximal coordination expressiveness, faithfully encoding all LINDA properties; it can implement articulated patterns such as a *barrier* among SC and users while other entities proceed concurrently.
- The authors attribute mainstream BCT limitations primarily to the choice of Object-Oriented Programming as the SC computational model (objects do not encapsulate control flow and interact only via synchronous request–response), compounded by strict termination requirements. The remedy is an *agent-oriented* paradigm with asynchronous messaging and proactivity (the three differentiating mechanisms: control-flow encapsulation, asynchronous communication, and pro-activeness).
- The paper argues for two broader benefits of on-chain LINDA-style coordination: *on-chain coordination* (a SC acting as a tuple space lets even other SC reserve/release resources via `in`/`out` with suspensive semantics, making automating agents publicly scrutinizable/trustworthy) and *accountable interaction* (LINDA's minimal primitive set fixes the finest, uniform granularity at which interactions are tracked on-chain, rather than leaving it to each developer).

## Conclusion

The study's central claim is supported: coordination expressiveness varies across blockchains and is not to be taken for granted, as demonstrated by the LINDA benchmark. Of the four BCT, only Tenderfone reached maximal expressiveness along the interaction dimension; Ethereum and HLF rank just below it (failing only full suspensive semantics), and Corda ranks lowest (also losing reference uncoupling due to its privacy mechanisms). The decisive enablers were asynchronous, agent-oriented message-passing, control-flow encapsulation, and proactive SC — features absent from mainstream OOP-based, termination-constrained SC, and especially limiting for inter-SC coordination. The authors acknowledge limitations: direct quantitative comparison with related blockchain-coordination approaches is currently impossible because of missing/incomplete benchmark implementations and radically differing methodologies; the implementations presented are proof-of-concept; and the broader on-chain-coordination and accountable-interaction benefits are argued conceptually rather than empirically validated. As future research, they propose encoding state-of-the-art coordination protocols and patterns on top of the Tenderfone LINDA implementation and demonstrating their effectiveness in orchestrating real-world computations in a reference scenario such as supply chain. The Related Works discussion also flags an open, recurring problem worth pursuing: how interacting agents avoid polling and instead suspend/get notified when tasks become enabled — precisely the suspensive-semantics challenge their benchmark targets.
