## Overview

This paper conducts the first bibliometric-content analysis of AI and blockchain integration specifically as it applies to business, addressing the gap that prior reviews either covered only one direction of the AI-blockchain relationship or did not connect the technical integration to business value. Starting from a Scopus search of 872 articles (AI-related AND blockchain-related keywords, articles/reviews, English, up to 2020), the authors filtered down to 106 articles that explicitly discuss business applications and benefits of integrated AI-blockchain platforms (excluding 90 papers covering the technologies separately and 676 covering only technical integration without business framing). Using VOSviewer/Gephi for citation analysis, keyword co-occurrence, and bibliographic coupling, plus Python-generated word clouds, the study answers four research questions: publication productivity, most influential articles, most prominent topics/themes (four thematic clusters), and most promising business application areas (10 areas identified).

## Background

The paper situates itself against five prior reviews: Omohundro (2014) on AI in smart contracts/cryptocurrencies; Karafiloski and Mishev (2017) on blockchain for big data challenges; Dinh and Thai (2018), who framed the field as "AI for blockchain" vs. "blockchain for AI"; Salah et al. (2019), who reviewed blockchain-for-AI benefits (data security, business process efficiency, trust in robotic decisions, collective decision-making, decentralized intelligence); and Pandl et al. (2020), a systematic review of AI/distributed-ledger convergence covering both directions but relying on manual/subjective insights. It also draws on Salah et al. (2019) and Morrison (2016) for the framing of AI and blockchain as complementary "yin and yang" technologies (AI for understanding/recognizing/deciding; blockchain for executing/verifying/recording), and cites Donthu et al. (2021a) for the four-step bibliometric methodology (define aims/scope, choose techniques, collect data, conduct analysis/report findings).

## Key Points

- Publications on AI-blockchain integration for business appeared only from 2017 onward and grew sharply in 2019 (44) and 2020 (54), reflecting an emerging field.
- The most-cited articles are Mamoshina et al. (2018) on AI-blockchain convergence in healthcare/biomedical research (102 citations), Liu et al. (2019b) on deep reinforcement learning for blockchain-enabled IIoT performance optimization (47 citations), and Mao et al. (2018) on an LSTM/blockchain-based credit evaluation system for food supply chains (44 citations).
- Keyword co-occurrence analysis shows "blockchain" (74 occurrences), "artificial intelligence" (31), "smart contract" (22), "machine learning" (19), and "internet of things" (13) as the most prominent topics; "blockchain–machine learning" is the strongest keyword pairing (weight 16), followed by "blockchain–smart contract" (9) and "blockchain–security" (7).
- Bibliographic coupling reveals four major thematic clusters covering >70% of the corpus: Cluster 1 "IR 4.0 and Supply Chains" (25 articles, 471 citations, generic/cross-industry), Cluster 2 "Smart Healthcare" (24 articles, 259 citations), Cluster 3 "Secure Transactions" (18 articles, 119 citations, generic), and Cluster 4 "Finance and Accounting" (14 articles, 240 citations).
- Cluster 1 examples include PriModChain (Arachchige et al., 2020 - Ethereum, smart contracts, federated ML, differential privacy for IIoT data trust), a three-layer blockchain-enabled cyber-physical system (Lee et al., 2019), and a blockchain/ML/IoT production capability evaluation system (Li et al., 2020).
- Cluster 2 examples include neural-network/error-backpropagation blockchain frameworks for medical data integrity (Kim and Huh, 2020), GuardHealth's consortium blockchain plus graph convolution network (Wang et al., 2020), and Mamoshina et al. (2018)'s patient-controlled health data marketplace.
- Cluster 3 examples include de-anonymizing Bitcoin via supervised ML/gradient boosting (Sun Yin et al., 2019), predictive policing via ML on Bitcoin blockchain data (Irwin and Turner, 2018), and ML/blockchain/IoT/signature-verification for online transaction identity theft mitigation (Jain et al., 2019).
- Cluster 4 examples include FinTech 3.0's integration of AI, big data, blockchain and cloud (Zheng et al., 2019), DeepBreath's CNN/deep-RL/blockchain financial portfolio optimization (Soleymani and Paquet, 2020), and smart-contract/AI approaches to preventing corporate fraud and credit risk (Roszkowska, 2020; Mao et al., 2018).
- The content analysis identifies 10 promising business application areas for AI-blockchain integration: e-commerce, finance and accounting, healthcare, intellectual property rights, management, marketing, smart manufacturing, social media, supply chain, and transportation, each illustrated with concrete exemplars (e.g., automated insurance fraud detection, IPR notarization via blockchain plus ML-based comparison, corporate online dispute resolution, customer satisfaction prediction via LSTM, misinformation control via blockchain proof-of-work plus ML classification).

## Conclusion

The study confirms that AI-blockchain integration for business is an emerging but rapidly growing research field (publications proliferating from 2019), with a small number of thematic clusters (four) indicating the field's relative immaturity and substantial room for growth. It concludes that AI and blockchain are complementary technologies whose combination addresses each other's weaknesses (AI's trustworthiness/privacy/explainability issues vs. blockchain's security/scalability issues), enabling decentralized, autonomous business models. The paper acknowledges limitations tied to reliance on Scopus data (potential database errors, mitigated via data cleaning per Donthu et al., 2021a) and the dynamic, fast-evolving nature of the field, meaning new developments may not be captured. It proposes an extensive table of future research questions (Table 11) spanning all four thematic clusters, covering topics such as which business processes/firm sizes benefit most, human capabilities needed for adoption, diffusion strategies, reskilling needs, managerial decision impacts, ethical/privacy issues, and metrics for monitoring AI-blockchain integration effectiveness.
