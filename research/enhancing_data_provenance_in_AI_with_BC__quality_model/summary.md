## Overview

This paper addresses the problem of ensuring trustworthiness and reliability of AI systems by focusing on "data provenance" — the ability to trace the source and history of data used to train and operate AI models. Its central contribution is a comprehensive quality model for blockchain-based data provenance, built by adapting the ISO/IEC 9126 software quality standard (Functionality, Reliability, Usability, Efficiency, Maintainability, Portability) to the unique characteristics of blockchain technology. For each of the six ISO/IEC 9126 characteristics, the authors define blockchain-specific sub-attributes (e.g., Data Immutability, Decentralized Ownership, Smart Contract Compliance, Transparency and Auditability, Data Integrity, Blockchain Security, Consensus Mechanism Reliability, Interoperability, Scalability, Energy Efficiency, Governance Framework, Cross-Platform Support) along with quantitative formulas for measuring each. The paper validates the model through a healthcare case study using the publicly available EyePACS Diabetic Retinopathy dataset, simulating how blockchain mechanisms would perform when applied to provenance control and data quality verification in a diagnostic AI workflow involving ~10,000 retinal images shared across multiple hospital stakeholders. The work positions itself as bridging a gap in the literature: prior blockchain-provenance studies focus on tracking data once it is on-chain, but none address evaluating data quality and provenance using a recognized software quality framework before/alongside blockchain integration.

## Background

The paper situates itself within a substantial existing body of research on blockchain-based data provenance, which it surveys in a literature table (Table 1) covering roughly 20 prior works. It cites Lautert et al. as advocating blockchain as a secure, transparent storage solution for provenance records; Lüthi et al. for introducing a graph-based provenance model and smart contract protocol for tracing AI assets; Ramachandran and Kantarcioglu for a smart-contract-based immutable data provenance management framework; and Sigwart et al. for blockchain-based provenance frameworks tailored to IoT's diverse data needs. Other cited works span IoT provenance frameworks (Yin and Fu; Javaid, Aman, and Sikdar; Siddiqui et al.'s BlockTrack-L), cloud-based provenance (Shetty et al.; Tosh et al.'s BlockCloud with Proof-of-Stake), healthcare-specific provenance (D'Antonio and Uccello), wireless sensor networks (Zeng et al.), fine-grained provenance systems like LineageChain on Hyperledger (Ruan et al.), AI pedigree verification on Hyperledger Fabric (Kawamoto and Kobayashi), and responsible/fair AI data provenance (Werder, Ramesh, and Zhang). The paper also draws on the ISO/IEC 9126 software quality model (citing Jalbani's earlier dissertation work on UML model quality assessment) as its foundational framework, and references regulatory context such as the GDPR (Voigt and Von dem Bussche) and Australian Privacy Principles to motivate why data provenance matters for compliance. It also cites Mougayar's characterization of blockchain's "garbage in, garbage out" limitation — that blockchain secures data immutably but cannot itself verify the truthfulness of data at the point of entry — as a key motivating concern. The healthcare case study is grounded in the EyePACS Diabetic Retinopathy Dataset from the Kaggle Diabetic Retinopathy Detection competition.

## Key Points

- The paper proposes a novel quality model for blockchain-based AI data provenance that adapts the six ISO/IEC 9126 quality characteristics (Functionality, Reliability, Usability, Efficiency, Maintainability, Portability) into blockchain-specific sub-attributes.
- Under Functionality, the model defines and provides quantitative formulas for Data Immutability (DI), Decentralized Ownership (DO, measured via Gini coefficient), Smart Contract Compliance (SCC), and Transparency and Auditability (TA, measured via average transaction retrieval time).
- Under Reliability, the model defines Data Integrity (percentage of records without anomalies), Blockchain Security (percentage of security incidents prevented), and Consensus Mechanism Reliability (percentage of successfully confirmed transactions).
- Under Usability, the model defines User-Friendly Interface for DApps (UFI-DApps, based on user survey scores), Accessibility (ACC, based on adherence to accessibility standards), and Interoperability (INT, based on successful integrations with external systems).
- Under Efficiency, the model defines Scalability (SC), Energy Efficiency (EE, energy consumed per transaction/work unit), and Transaction Throughput (TT, transactions processed per unit time).
- Under Maintainability, the model defines Upgradability (UP, downtime during upgrades), Governance Framework (GF, percentage of successful governance decisions), and Interoperability with Other Blockchains (IOB).
- Under Portability, the model defines Blockchain Compatibility (BC), Cross-Platform Support (CPS), and Interoperability with the DLT Ecosystem (IDE).
- The paper claims that existing literature has not previously integrated the ISO/IEC 9126 software quality model with blockchain-based data provenance, framing this integration as the paper's primary novel contribution and gap-filling move.
- The paper presents a case study applying the full quality model to a simulated diabetic retinopathy diagnostic AI pipeline using the EyePACS dataset (~10,000 retinal images, 1,800 smart contract instances), reporting specific quantitative outcomes: 100% data immutability, a Gini coefficient of 0.12 for decentralized ownership, 98.3% smart contract compliance, 99.95% data integrity, 99.7% consensus confirmation rate (via Delegated Proof-of-Stake), a 4.6/5 usability score from a simulated 50-professional survey, transaction throughput of 4,500 records/hour, an estimated 18% energy reduction versus conventional cloud systems, 100% node participation with zero downtime during a governance-approved upgrade, and successful cross-platform operation across Linux and Windows.
- The paper argues that these case-study results support the conceptual viability of blockchain, when combined with an ISO/IEC 9126-aligned quality model, as a mechanism for enhancing trust, transparency, and regulatory compliance (e.g., GDPR, APP) in AI data infrastructures, particularly in healthcare.

## Conclusion

The paper concludes that its blockchain-enabled, ISO/IEC 9126-aligned quality model provides a structured, quantifiable methodology for assessing the trustworthiness and operational readiness of AI data infrastructures, and that the healthcare case study demonstrates the model's practical applicability across all six quality dimensions. The reported results (near-perfect immutability, integrity, and compliance scores; strong usability and energy efficiency) are presented as evidence supporting the hypothesis that blockchain coupled with this quality framework can enhance AI data provenance. However, the authors explicitly acknowledge limitations: the case study is based on a simulated application of blockchain mechanisms to a public dataset (EyePACS) rather than a deployed, real-world blockchain system with live operational data — several figures (e.g., the user survey of 50 professionals, the 1,800 smart contract instances, governance voting outcomes) appear to be illustrative/assumed rather than measured from an actual implementation. The paper explicitly calls for future work including real-world pilots that capture live metrics, extension of the quality framework to dynamic governance and ongoing auditability, development of adaptive smart contracts for automated quality governance, and exploration of cross-sector applications beyond healthcare. Overall, the paper frames itself as establishing conceptual and methodological groundwork — a "new standard" for AI data quality assurance grounded in both an established software quality standard and blockchain technology — rather than delivering a fully validated, deployed system.
